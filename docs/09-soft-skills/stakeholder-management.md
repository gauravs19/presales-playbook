Every enterprise deal involves multiple stakeholders, often with conflicting interests. You must quickly categorize them to adapt your presentation style.

### 1. The Economic Buyer
- **Who they are:** The person with ultimate financial authority (e.g., CIO, VP of Engineering, CFO).
- **What they care about:** ROI, Time-to-Value, total cost of ownership (TCO), and mitigating strategic risk.
- **How to engage:** Keep it high-level. Do not show them code snippets or dive into API endpoints unless explicitly asked. Focus entirely on business outcomes. How does this solve their strategic problem and save them money?

### 2. The Champion
- **Who they are:** Someone inside the organization who deeply understands the pain, believes your solution is the answer, and actively sells on your behalf when you aren't in the room. They have influence but not necessarily the final budget.
- **What they care about:** Solving their daily pain, looking good to their boss, career advancement.
- **How to engage:** Arm them to the teeth. Give them custom ROI calculators, specific slide decks, and competitive battlecards they can use internally to secure budget and executive sponsorship.

### 3. The Technical Evaluator (The Coach)
- **Who they are:** Lead Architects, Senior Developers, or Engineering Managers tasked with vetting the technology.
- **What they care about:** Scalability, security, API extensibility, developer experience (DevEx), and avoiding technical debt.
- **How to engage:** Speak their language. Be authentic and transparent. If your tool doesn't do something well, admit it and present a workaround. Earning their respect is critical; if they veto your tech, the deal is dead.

### 4. The End User
- **Who they are:** The individuals who will log into your system every day.
- **What they care about:** Ease of use, automation, reducing manual toil.
- **How to engage:** Show them specifically how your solution makes their Friday afternoons easier. Focus on UI, workflows, and integrations with their existing tools (e.g., Slack, Jira).

### 5. The Detractor (The "Blocker")
- **Who they are:** Someone fundamentally opposed to your solution. They might prefer a competitor, want to build it in-house, or fear your tool will automate away their job.
- **What they care about:** Job security, maintaining their current status quo.
- **How to engage:** Identify them early through your Champion. Do not argue with them in public meetings. Take them offline. Try to uncover the root cause of their resistance and reposition your tool as something that elevates their role, rather than replacing it.
