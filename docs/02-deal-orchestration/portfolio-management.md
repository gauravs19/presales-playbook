# 2.4 Portfolio Management

## 2.4.1 Tiering Your Deal Portfolio

You cannot invest equally in every deal and expect above-average results. The math doesn't allow it. If you have 12 active deals and 45 available hours per week for deal work, that's 3.75 hours per deal per week. For a complex enterprise deal that needs discovery, solution design, demo preparation, and POC execution, 3.75 hours is nothing. Your best deals are being starved because your worst deals are getting the same attention.

Tiering is the discipline of explicitly categorising your deals by investment worthiness and allocating your time accordingly.

1. **Tier 1: All-In (2–3 deals).** These are your highest-probability, highest-value deals. Strong champion, confirmed budget, clear timeline, strong technical fit. These deals get your best work: custom demos, deep solution design, proactive follow-ups, executive briefing preparation. Rule: Tier 1 deals get 60% of your time.

2. **Tier 2: Strategic Investment (3–4 deals).** These are real opportunities with one or two gaps — maybe the champion isn't fully confirmed, or the budget is in process, or the timeline is soft. These deals get solid work but not bespoke: tailored (not custom) demos, standard discovery depth, responsive (not proactive) follow-up. Rule: Tier 2 deals get 30% of your time.

3. **Tier 3: Monitor (3–5 deals).** These are early-stage, exploratory, or low-confidence deals. They might become real, but they're not there yet. These deals get minimal SE investment: attend meetings if scheduled, provide standard materials, but no custom preparation. Rule: Tier 3 deals get 10% of your time. If a Tier 3 deal doesn't progress to Tier 2 within 4 weeks, close it or hand it off.

4. **Re-tier weekly.** Deals move. A Tier 2 deal where the champion just got executive sponsorship becomes Tier 1. A Tier 1 deal where the budget just got frozen becomes Tier 3. Review your tier assignments every Monday morning. If you're not moving deals between tiers regularly, either your tiering criteria are too loose or your deals aren't progressing.

5. **Communicate your tiering to your AE.** AEs need to know where their deals stand in your portfolio: "I've tiered our deal portfolio for this quarter. [Company A] and [Company B] are my Tier 1 — they're getting full investment. [Company C] is Tier 2 — solid effort but not bespoke. [Company D] and [Company E] are Tier 3 — I'll attend meetings but won't do custom preparation unless something changes. If you want to move a deal up, help me close the qualification gaps." This transparency prevents conflict and gives the AE agency to improve deal quality.

> **War Story:** An SE was running 15 deals simultaneously and treating them all as Tier 1. Her average prep time per deal was 45 minutes per week. She was exhausted, her demo quality had dropped, and her technical win rate had fallen to 22%. Her manager required her to tier: 3 Tier 1, 4 Tier 2, 8 Tier 3. The result: her Tier 1 deals got 5–6 hours per week each. Her demo quality skyrocketed. Two of the three Tier 1 deals closed that quarter. Her technical win rate climbed to 50%. Total revenue influenced: higher than the previous quarter despite managing fewer deals intensively. She didn't become a better SE. She became a better allocator.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who refuses to tier because "every customer deserves my best effort." They're not wrong morally — but they're wrong strategically. Equal effort across unequal deals produces equally mediocre results. The customer on your Tier 1 deal who gets 3 hours of your time instead of 6 because you're spreading yourself across 15 deals is the customer being harmed by your egalitarianism.

**Interview Angle:**
"How do you manage your time across multiple deals?"
A strong answer describes a tiering system with specific criteria and time allocation rules. A weak answer says "I prioritise based on what's most urgent" — which is reactive, not strategic.

🟡 **Mid-Level** — Tiering is not about working less. It's about working smarter. The discipline to under-invest in low-probability deals is what creates the capacity to over-invest in high-probability ones.

## 2.4.2 The Weekly SE Deal Review

Most SE teams have a weekly pipeline review — but it's usually an AE-led meeting where SEs passively report on deal status. The Weekly SE Deal Review is different. It's an SE-led exercise where you audit your own portfolio with the discipline of a portfolio manager reviewing fund allocation.

This isn't about updating a spreadsheet. It's about making three decisions every Monday: what to invest more in, what to invest less in, and what to kill.

1. **Block 30 minutes every Monday morning.** Non-negotiable. Before you open email. Before you accept meeting requests. Before you start preparing for the week's calls. This 30-minute block is the most strategically valuable half-hour in your week. It's where you shift from reactive ("what meetings do I have today?") to proactive ("what deals need my attention this week, and what can I deprioritise?").

2. **Review each deal against four questions:**
   - **What changed since last week?** New stakeholder identified? Budget confirmed? Champion went dark? Competitor surfaced? If nothing changed, the deal is stalling — and stalling is dying in slow motion.
   - **What is the #1 risk to this deal right now?** Not the general risk — the specific, immediate risk. "No access to economic buyer" is more actionable than "deal might not close."
   - **What is the single most important action I can take this week?** Not a list of 5 tasks. One task. The one that moves the deal forward the most. "Send the executive architecture summary to the CTO" or "Run the POC results call with the security team."
   - **Should this deal's tier change?** Based on what's changed, does this deal deserve more or less of your time than it got last week?

3. **Flag stalled deals immediately.** If a deal has had "no change" for 2 consecutive weeks, it's stalling. Run the 3-touch requalification sequence (Section 2.2.2). If it doesn't respond, demote it to Tier 3 or close it. The most expensive deals in your portfolio are the ones that haven't moved in 3 weeks but haven't been officially killed.

4. **Share your review with your AE partners.** After your Monday audit, send a brief message to each AE: "Here's my status on our shared deals. [Deal A]: on track, next step is POC kickoff Wed. [Deal B]: stalling — no response from champion in 10 days. Can you try re-engaging? [Deal C]: strong trajectory, I'm prioritising this as Tier 1 this week." This 2-minute message prevents miscommunication and shows the AE that you're managing their deals with intentionality.

5. **Track trends, not just snapshots.** After 4 weeks of Monday reviews, patterns emerge: "My deals consistently stall after discovery but before demo — I might not be creating enough urgency in my discovery close." "I'm spending 40% of my time on Tier 3 deals that never progress — I need to be more aggressive about qualification." These patterns are invisible in a single review but obvious across a month of data.

> **War Story:** An SE started the Monday review ritual and within 3 weeks discovered something troubling: 4 of his 10 deals had been in the same stage for 3+ weeks. Not because they were complex — because nobody on either side (SE or AE) had taken the next step. No follow-up sent. No meeting scheduled. No action taken. They were "active" in the CRM but dead in reality. He killed 2 and reactivated 2 with direct outreach. His pipeline went from 10 deals at 30% average accuracy to 8 deals at 55% accuracy. His manager was initially concerned about the pipeline shrinkage. By quarter end, the 8 accurate deals produced more revenue than the 10 stale ones would have.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who "doesn't have time" for the Monday review. They jump straight into Monday's meetings, react to whatever the AE asks for, and spend the week in firefighting mode. By Friday, they can't remember which deals they advanced and which ones they neglected. They're perpetually reactive — doing what feels urgent instead of what is important. The 30-minute Monday review is the antidote to reactive presales.

**Interview Angle:**
"Walk me through your weekly routine for managing your deal portfolio."
A strong answer describes a structured review cadence with specific questions, tiering decisions, and AE communication. A weak answer describes "checking CRM and responding to emails."

## 2.4.3 Saying No to Low-Priority Requests

The ability to say no is the highest-leverage time management skill in presales. It's also the hardest — because the SE role is culturally wired to say yes. The AE asks for a demo: yes. The SDR asks for a quick technical call: yes. Product marketing asks for a customer reference story: yes. The SE manager asks you to mentor a new hire: yes. Each individual "yes" is reasonable. Collectively, they consume 30–40% of your week with work that doesn't move your top deals forward.

1. **Distinguish between urgent and important.** Most low-priority requests feel urgent because someone is asking you directly and they need an answer now. But urgent ≠ important. A Tier 3 deal demo is urgent (the AE wants it scheduled this week). A Tier 1 deal executive briefing is important (it determines whether your biggest deal advances). If you say yes to every urgent request, you'll never have time for the important ones. The solution isn't to be unhelpful — it's to triage.

2. **The "Yes, and here's the trade-off" technique.** Never respond to a request with a flat no. Respond with: "Yes, I can do that. If I do, here's what I'll need to deprioritise: [Tier 1 deal activity]. Are you comfortable with that trade-off?" This puts the decision back on the requestor. If the AE says "yes, please deprioritise the Tier 1 deal for my Tier 3 request," escalate to your SE manager — because that's a portfolio-level decision, not an individual request.

3. **Batch low-priority requests.** Some requests don't need customised attention — they need a standard response. Build a library of reusable assets: a standard demo recording, a pre-written architecture overview, a FAQ document for common technical questions. When a low-priority request comes in, check whether a reusable asset addresses it before committing custom time.

4. **Set expectations proactively.** At the start of each week, communicate your priorities to AE partners: "This week, my priority deals are [A] and [B]. I've blocked time for their executive briefing and POC prep. I can attend the [C] meeting on Thursday if it's 30 minutes or less, but I won't be doing custom preparation. If something changes on [C] that makes it more urgent, let me know and we'll re-tier." Proactive communication prevents reactive conflict.

5. **Protect your calendar architecturally.** Block 2–3 hours per day for Tier 1 deal work. Make these blocks visible on your calendar. When someone tries to schedule over them, point to the block: "I have that time reserved for [Tier 1 deal] preparation. Can we do 3 PM instead?" Calendar blocks are the physical manifestation of your tiering decisions. Without them, your time allocation is a wish, not a plan.

> **War Story:** An SE tracked his time for 2 weeks and discovered he was spending 12 hours per week on requests that didn't relate to his top 3 deals: ad-hoc demos for SDR-sourced leads, internal product feedback sessions, team meeting preparation, and "quick" technical calls that always ran 45 minutes. He implemented three changes: he redirected SDR demo requests to a pre-recorded demo library, he declined non-essential internal meetings, and he blocked 2 hours each morning for Tier 1 deal work. The result: he reclaimed 8 hours per week — essentially a full day — that he redirected to his Tier 1 deals. His technical win rate climbed from 35% to 48% that quarter. He didn't work more. He said no more.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who says yes to everything because they're afraid of being perceived as unhelpful or not a "team player." They attend every meeting, accept every request, and burn out by mid-quarter. Their Tier 1 deals suffer because they never have deep work time. Their AEs are dissatisfied because the custom demo they received was rushed. Their manager is concerned because their win rate is dropping. The irony: saying yes to everything makes everyone less satisfied, not more.

**Interview Angle:**
"How do you handle situations where you have more requests than bandwidth?"
A strong answer describes a triage system (tiering, trade-off communication, reusable assets). A weak answer says "I work extra hours" or "I try to fit everything in."

**Practical Reference: The Request Triage Flowchart**

```
New request received
  ├── Does it serve a Tier 1 or Tier 2 deal?
  │     ├── Yes → Accept and schedule
  │     └── No → Continue evaluation
  │
  ├── Can a reusable asset address it?
  │     ├── Yes → Send the asset, no custom work
  │     └── No → Continue evaluation
  │
  ├── Will saying yes require deprioritising a Tier 1 activity?
  │     ├── Yes → Communicate trade-off to requestor
  │     └── No → Accept if time permits
  │
  └── Can it wait until next week?
        ├── Yes → Defer with clear timeline
        └── No → Escalate to SE manager for prioritisation
```

🟡 **Mid-Level** — Saying no is an investment in your yes. Every no to a low-priority request is a yes to your highest-value work.

## 2.4.4 Preventing Your Best Deals From Being Starved

The paradox of enterprise presales: your best deals — the ones with the highest probability and the largest value — are the ones most likely to be under-serviced. Not because you don't care about them. Because everyone else's urgency steals your attention. The Tier 3 deal that needs a "quick demo tomorrow." The AE who needs "just 30 minutes" for a call prep. The product team that needs feedback "by end of day." Each interruption is individually harmless. Collectively, they starve your best deals of the deep work they require.

Deal starvation is the silent killer of SE revenue contribution. You won't see it in your CRM. You'll see it in your win rate — specifically, the deals you lost not because the competition was better, but because you didn't give your best deal your best effort.

1. **Recognise the starvation symptoms.** Your Tier 1 deal prep happens at 10 PM because your daytime was consumed by lower-priority work. Your demo for the most important deal is "good enough" instead of "exceptional." Your follow-up takes 3 days instead of 24 hours because you were putting out fires. Your executive briefing slides are repurposed from a previous deal instead of customised. If any of these are happening, your best deals are starving.

2. **Block "deep work" time and defend it aggressively.** For every Tier 1 deal, block a specific 2–3 hour window each week for concentrated preparation. Label the block with the deal name on your calendar. When someone tries to schedule over it: "That time is reserved for [Company A] executive briefing prep. Can we find an alternative time?" This isn't being difficult. This is being disciplined. The SE who can't protect 2 hours for their most important deal is managing their calendar with the same discipline they'd criticise in a prospect's project management.

3. **Quantify the cost of starvation.** When you're tempted to skip your Tier 1 prep for a Tier 3 request, do the math. Your Tier 1 deal is worth $500K. Your Tier 3 deal is worth $75K with a 20% win probability (expected value: $15K). The 2 hours you'd spend on the Tier 3 demo would be better invested in the Tier 1 executive briefing — where 2 hours of preparation could be the difference between a compelling presentation and a forgettable one. This isn't speculation. Over a career, the SEs who consistently invest their best effort in their best deals produce 2–3x the revenue of SEs who spread effort equally.

4. **Make your best deals your default mode.** Your default activity — what you work on when nothing is scheduled — should be advancing your Tier 1 deals. Not email. Not Slack. Not CRM updates. Your Tier 1 deal always has a next action, and that action should be what you do first, every day. Everything else fits around it.

5. **Report starvation to your manager.** If structural issues are starving your best deals — too many deals assigned, too many non-deal requests, too many internal obligations — raise it explicitly: "My Tier 1 deals are getting 4 hours per week each. Based on their complexity, they need 6–8. I'm being pulled into [specific low-value activities] that are consuming the difference. Can we discuss how to protect my bandwidth on my top opportunities?" This isn't complaining. This is managing up — and it's a Senior SE responsibility.

> **War Story:** An SE was running a $1.1M Tier 1 deal alongside 9 other deals. Her Tier 1 deal required a custom architecture review for the prospect's CTO — a document that would take 5–6 hours to produce well. Over 3 consecutive days, she got pulled into: an emergency demo for a Tier 3 deal (the AE's manager pressured her), a product feedback session with engineering, and a team lunch-and-learn she was presenting. By Thursday, she'd spent zero hours on the architecture review. She produced it Friday afternoon in 2 hours — a rushed version that was "fine" but not compelling. The CTO's feedback: "Your competitor's architecture document was more thorough." That document gap contributed to a competitive loss on a $1.1M deal. Three low-priority interruptions over three days cost her company seven figures.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who operates on a "whoever asks first" model. Their time goes to whoever sends the first Slack message or schedules the first meeting. This isn't a time management strategy — it's the absence of one. The result is chronically starved Tier 1 deals and well-fed Tier 3 deals — an exact inversion of optimal resource allocation.

**Interview Angle:**
"How do you protect your time for your most important deals?"
A strong answer describes specific techniques (calendar blocking, tiering, trade-off communication, manager escalation). A weak answer describes being "really busy" without demonstrating strategic prioritisation.

**Practical Reference: The Deal Starvation Prevention Checklist**

| Check | Healthy | Starved |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Tier 1 prep time per week | 5–8 hours per deal | < 3 hours per deal |
| Follow-up response time | < 24 hours | > 48 hours |
| Demo/briefing quality | Customised, compelling | Repurposed, adequate |
| Deep work blocks on calendar | 2–3 per week, protected | None, or frequently overridden |
| Default activity | Tier 1 deal advancement | Email, Slack, ad-hoc requests |

**If 3+ rows show "Starved," your best deals are at risk. Act this week.**

🟡 **Mid-Level** — Your best deals deserve your best work. Not your leftover work. Not your late-night work. Your *best* work, during your *best* hours, with your *full* attention.
