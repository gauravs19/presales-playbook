# 0.4 How to Learn From Senior SEs

## 0.4.1 The Art of Strategic Shadowing

Most SE teams will tell a new hire to "shadow some calls." What they won't tell you is *how* to shadow effectively. Sitting on a Zoom call with your camera off while checking Slack is not shadowing — it's attendance. Strategic shadowing is a deliberate practice that turns observation into skill acquisition at 3x the speed of passive exposure.

1. **Pick your target wisely.** Not all senior SEs are equally worth shadowing. The SE with the highest win rate might be operating on pure charisma and relationship capital — skills that are hard to reverse-engineer. The SE who wins deals from behind (converting sceptics, navigating complex architectures, recovering from bad demos) teaches you far more useful mechanics. Ask your manager: "Who is the best SE at winning deals that should have been lost?" That's who you shadow first.

2. **Declare your focus before the call.** Tell the senior SE: "I'm specifically watching how you handle technical objections today." This does two things: it forces you to observe with intention, and it gives the senior SE a frame for the post-call debrief. Without a declared focus, you'll try to absorb everything and retain nothing.

3. **Count, don't just watch.** During the call, track quantitative signals: How many questions did the SE ask versus answer? How many seconds of silence followed a hard question? How many times did the SE redirect the conversation versus follow the prospect's lead? These numbers reveal patterns that "it felt like they did well" never will.

4. **Record the pivots, not the scripts.** The most valuable moments in any customer call are the *pivots* — the moments where the conversation went somewhere unexpected and the SE made a real-time decision about how to respond. Did the prospect suddenly ask about a competitor? Did the CTO raise a concern that wasn't in the briefing? How did the SE handle it? These pivot moments are where expertise lives.

5. **Shadow across different AEs, not just different SEs.** The same senior SE behaves differently with different AEs. With a strong AE, they play a supporting role and let the AE drive. With a weak AE, they quietly take control of the meeting flow. Observing the same SE across 3 different AE relationships teaches you adaptability — the most underrated skill in presales.

> **War Story:** A new SE shadowed 15 calls in her first month. She didn't debrief any of them. When asked what she learned, she said, "I saw how the team handles demos and discovery." When pressed for specifics, she couldn't name a single technique, pivot, or decision point. She'd been present for 15+ hours of live presales work and retained almost nothing actionable. Compare that to her peer who shadowed 6 calls, debriefed each one, and could articulate exactly why the senior SE paused for 4 seconds after the CTO said "we're also talking to Snowflake." Six productive shadows beat fifteen passive ones.

> **Failure Mode:** The new SE who only shadows SEs they "click with" personally. They avoid the abrasive-but-brilliant SE who is the best deal closer on the team. They shadow the friendly SE who runs smooth meetings but has a mediocre win rate. They learn to be pleasant. They don't learn to win.

**Interview Angle:**
"What's the most useful thing you've ever learned from watching a more experienced SE work?"
A strong answer identifies a specific technique or decision point and explains how they incorporated it. A weak answer gives a general compliment without specifics.

**Practical Reference: The Shadowing Impact Matrix**
After 10 shadow sessions, categorise your observations:

| Category | Techniques Observed | Applied to My Own Deals? |
|----------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Discovery questioning | e.g., "The pause after asking about budget" | Yes — tried it, prospect opened up more |
| Objection handling | e.g., "Validated before redirecting" | Not yet — need to practise |
| Meeting control | e.g., "Agenda-setting in first 2 minutes" | Yes — reduced meeting drift |
| Competitive positioning | e.g., "Never named competitor directly" | Observing more before trying |

🟢 **Beginner** — Strategic shadowing has diminishing returns after about 20 well-debriefed sessions. After that, you learn more from leading than observing. Know when to transition.

## 0.4.2 How to Ask for Feedback Without Being Dismissed

Senior SEs are busy. They're running their own deals, preparing for demos, writing POC criteria, and fighting for bandwidth. When a new SE walks up and asks, "Do you have any feedback for me?", the answer will always be "you're doing great, keep it up." That's not feedback. That's a polite dismissal because you asked a question that requires too much effort to answer properly.

Learning to *extract* useful feedback from busy experts is a meta-skill that will serve you for your entire career.

1. **Be specific in your ask.** Never ask "do you have feedback?" Instead, ask: "In the demo I ran yesterday, I noticed the prospect went quiet after I showed the reporting module. Did I lose them? What would you have done differently in that segment?" Specific questions get specific answers. Vague questions get vague answers.

2. **Make it easy to say something critical.** Senior SEs don't want to crush your confidence, so they default to positive feedback. Lower the barrier by framing your ask as: "If you had to pick one thing I should stop doing in customer calls, what would it be?" The word "one" makes it manageable. The word "stop" gives them permission to be critical without feeling like they're attacking you.

3. **Time it right.** Immediately after a customer call — within 5 minutes — is the best time to ask for feedback. The call is still fresh. Specific moments are still in memory. If you wait until the weekly team meeting, the details have faded and you'll get generic advice instead of tactical coaching.

4. **Act on it visibly.** If a senior SE tells you to "slow down your demo transitions," make a visible effort in the next meeting to slow down — and then tell them: "I consciously paused between sections today because of your feedback. Did it land better?" This closes the feedback loop and signals that their time investment was worthwhile. They'll give you better feedback next time because they know it actually gets used.

5. **Don't argue with feedback.** When someone tells you that your discovery was too surface-level, your instinct will be to defend: "But the prospect wasn't giving me detailed answers." Resist that instinct. Say: "That's fair. What question would you have asked to go deeper?" Feedback is a gift, even when it stings. Arguing with it ensures you'll never receive it again.

> **War Story:** A new SE asked their mentor, "How's my demo?" The mentor said, "It's good, you know the product well." Three months later, the same SE asked a different question: "I watched the recording of my demo from Tuesday. At minute 8, I showed three features back-to-back without connecting any of them to the prospect's stated pain. Would you have stopped after the first feature and asked a checking question?" The mentor's face lit up. They spent 20 minutes breaking down demo pacing, checking questions, and how to create "micro-confirmations" throughout a presentation. The quality of the question determined the quality of the answer.

> **Failure Mode:** The new SE who receives critical feedback and never asks that person again. They felt embarrassed when a senior SE said "your discovery questions were too leading — you were telling the prospect what their problem is instead of letting them tell you." Instead of processing the feedback and adjusting, they avoided that SE and started asking a friendlier colleague who only ever said "you're doing great." Their discovery skills never improved. They hit a wall at month 6 and couldn't figure out why.

**Interview Angle:**
"How do you seek and process constructive criticism?"
A strong answer demonstrates a proactive, specific feedback-seeking habit and gives an example of feedback that changed their behaviour. A weak answer says "I'm always open to feedback" without a specific example.

**Practical Reference: The Feedback Request Template**
Use this structure every time you ask for feedback:

*"Hey [Name], quick feedback request from [specific meeting/demo]. At [specific moment], I [specific action]. I'm not sure it landed well because [specific observation]. If you were in my position, what would you have done differently at that moment?"*

This structure works because it shows you've already reflected, reduces the senior SE's effort, and focuses on a single actionable moment.

🟢 **Beginner** — The quality of feedback you receive is directly proportional to the quality of the question you ask. Invest in your questions.

## 0.4.3 Reverse-Engineering a Senior SE's Deal Approach

The best presales education isn't in a training module. It's in the historical record of deals that skilled SEs have already won and lost. Every closed deal is a case study. Every lost deal is a post-mortem. And most of this intelligence is sitting in your CRM, in recorded calls, and in Slack threads — unread and unanalysed by anyone except the SE who lived through it.

Here's how to reverse-engineer a senior SE's approach systematically:

1. **Pick 3 closed-won deals and read the full CRM history.** Choose deals from the senior SE whose approach you most want to learn. Read every activity log, every note, every email attachment. Map the timeline: when did discovery happen? How many meetings before the demo? Was there a POC? How long was negotiation? You're looking for the *sequencing* of their approach, not just the content.

2. **Listen to their recorded calls.** If your company uses Gong, Chorus, or any call recording platform, go back and listen to 3–4 key calls from a successful deal. Listen for: how they open (do they start with an agenda or a question?), how they respond to objections (validate first or counter immediately?), and how they close (firm next step or open-ended?). Take timestamped notes on specific techniques.

3. **Map their stakeholder engagement pattern.** In the CRM, check how many different people the senior SE engaged. Did they meet only with the engineering team, or did they also engage the business sponsor? Did they request a separate session with the security team? The breadth of their engagement tells you whether they're a single-threaded or multi-threaded operator.

4. **Identify the deal's inflection point.** Every won deal has a moment where it shifted from "maybe" to "probably." It might be a successful POC checkpoint, a key objection that was resolved, or a meeting with the economic buyer that the champion arranged. Find that moment. Understanding *what caused the shift* is the highest-value insight in deal analysis.

5. **Compare with a closed-lost deal from the same SE.** Now pick a deal the same SE lost. Run the same analysis. Where did they deviate from their usual pattern? Was discovery shallower? Were fewer stakeholders engaged? Was the inflection point missing? Comparing wins and losses from the same SE reveals their success formula more clearly than studying wins alone.

> **War Story:** A new SE reverse-engineered 5 deals from the team's top closer. She noticed a pattern nobody had articulated: in every closed-won deal, the senior SE had a separate, off-calendar conversation with the prospect's IT operations lead — not the architect, not the engineering manager, but the person responsible for running the system after deployment. In every closed-lost deal, this conversation never happened. The operations lead was the silent stakeholder who could block procurement with a single "I don't feel comfortable supporting this." The senior SE had never formalised this step. It was instinct. The new SE formalised it, added "Operations Stakeholder Conversation" to her personal discovery checklist, and improved her first-year win rate by 12 percentage points.

> **Failure Mode:** The new SE who tries to replicate a senior SE's style instead of their structure. They mimic the confident delivery, the casual humour, the authoritative tone — but they skip the preparation, the stakeholder mapping, and the post-call follow-up that the senior SE does invisibly behind the scenes. They become a convincing performer who loses deals because the substance isn't there.

**Interview Angle:**
"How do you learn from deals that other SEs on your team have closed?"
A strong answer describes a systematic approach — CRM review, call recording analysis, pattern extraction. A weak answer says "we have team meetings where we share updates."

## 0.4.4 When You Know More Than the Senior SE

It will happen sooner than you expect. You'll be sitting in a meeting and a prospect will ask about a Kubernetes orchestration pattern, a GraphQL federation approach, or a machine learning pipeline architecture — and you'll know the answer. The senior SE won't. They'll either bluff, deflect, or look at you with the universal "help me" expression that every SE learns to recognise.

This moment is a minefield. Handle it well and you become an indispensable complement to the senior team. Handle it badly and you become "that junior SE who made Sarah look bad in front of the customer."

1. **Never correct a senior SE in front of a customer.** This is the cardinal rule and it has no exceptions. Even if the senior SE just told the prospect something factually incorrect, do not jump in with "actually, that's not quite right." The prospect's trust in your team evaporates the moment they see internal disagreement. Wait until after the call. Address it privately. If the error could cause a material problem (a false compliance claim, an impossible performance guarantee), message the SE privately during the call: "Hey, I want to make sure we're precise on that point — want me to clarify?" Let them invite you in.

2. **Use the "add-on" technique, not the "correction" technique.** If you need to contribute your expertise in real time, frame it as an addition: "Building on what Sarah said, one thing I'd add specifically for your Kubernetes environment is..." This positions you as deepening the answer, not contradicting it. The senior SE looks good because their answer is being "expanded upon" rather than "fixed."

3. **Offer your expertise in advance, not in the moment.** If you know you have domain knowledge the senior SE lacks, brief them *before* the meeting: "Hey, I noticed the prospect's architecture section mentions gRPC service mesh. I've worked with Istio and Linkerd extensively — want me to take the integration questions if they come up?" This lets the senior SE delegate gracefully instead of being surprised.

4. **Document your contributions without self-promotion.** After a call where your technical depth was critical, don't send a Slack message saying "I really saved that meeting when the Kafka question came up." Instead, log detailed technical notes in the CRM and let the record speak for itself. Your manager will notice. The senior SE will notice. The contribution is visible without you pointing at it.

5. **Build a reputation as the team's specialist, not a threat.** If you have deep expertise in cloud architecture, ML pipelines, or security — make yourself the team's go-to resource for that domain. When other SEs get questions they can't answer, they should think of you first. Being the specialist everyone calls upon is the fastest path to influence and respect. Being the junior who corrects seniors is the fastest path to isolation.

> **War Story:** A new SE with a background in data engineering joined a team where the senior SEs were generalists. In a customer meeting, the prospect's data architect asked about Change Data Capture (CDC) patterns for real-time replication from Oracle to Snowflake. The senior SE gave a vague answer about "our platform handles data movement." The new SE could see the data architect losing interest — the answer was too shallow for a technical evaluator. The new SE messaged the senior SE: "Want me to take this one? I've implemented CDC with Debezium in production." The senior SE said, "Please." The new SE spent 5 minutes walking through CDC architecture specific to the prospect's Oracle version. The data architect said, "That's the first time a vendor has actually understood our replication challenge." The deal progressed. The senior SE thanked the new SE publicly at the team meeting. The new SE became the team's go-to for all data integration conversations — three weeks into the job.

> **Failure Mode:** The new SE who, knowing more about a specific technology, starts subtly undermining the senior SE's credibility. They sigh audibly when the senior SE gives a simplified answer. They send "helpful" articles to the senior SE after meetings. They tell AEs, "You should really bring me into that deal instead of Sarah — I know the technology better." Within weeks, they've earned a reputation as arrogant. The senior SE stops inviting them to calls. AEs don't request their involvement. Their technical brilliance becomes irrelevant because nobody wants to work with them.

**Interview Angle:**
"Tell me about a time you knew more about a topic than a senior colleague in a customer meeting. How did you handle it?"
A strong answer demonstrates restraint, collaboration, and a focus on the deal outcome rather than personal recognition. A weak answer focuses on how they were right and how the senior person was wrong.

**Practical Reference: The Expertise Contribution Framework**

| Situation | Do This | Don't Do This |
|-----------|---------|---------------|
| Senior SE gives a vague answer | Message privately, offer to take the question | Correct them publicly |
| You have domain expertise coming into the meeting | Brief the senior SE beforehand | Wait for them to struggle, then rescue |
| Your contribution was critical to the deal | Log detailed notes in CRM | Send a team Slack highlighting your save |
| Senior SE asks for help mid-call | Accept gracefully, deliver cleanly | Make it obvious they needed help |

🟢 **Beginner** — At this level, knowing when to speak and when to stay silent is more important than what you actually know. Technical superiority without political intelligence is a net negative.
