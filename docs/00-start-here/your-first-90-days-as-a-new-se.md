# 0.3 Your First 90 Days as a New SE

## 0.3.1 Week 1-2: Orient Before You Operate

The fastest way to fail in a new SE role is to try to add value too quickly. You're hired for your potential and your adjacent experience. You are not yet hired for your judgement — that takes time to build in context. Your first two weeks should be spent absorbing, not performing.

Here's your orientation protocol:

1. **Map the product landscape.** Don't try to learn every feature. Instead, learn the *five use cases* that close the most deals. Ask your SE manager or a senior SE: "What are the top five things we demo most often, and why do customers buy?" These five use cases will cover 80% of your first-quarter activity. Learn them cold. Everything else can wait.

2. **Read the last 10 closed-won deals.** Open the CRM and read the activity history of 10 recently closed deals. For each one, note: what was the prospect's pain, what did we demo, did we do a POC, how long was the cycle, and who was the champion? This gives you pattern recognition faster than any training deck ever could. You're looking for the *shape* of a successful deal, not the details.

3. **Meet every function, not just sales.** Schedule 30-minute coffee chats with one person from each adjacent team: Product, Engineering, Professional Services, Customer Success, Marketing. Ask each of them: "What does presales do well here, and what frustrates you about working with the SE team?" The answers will tell you more about your company's presales culture than your manager ever will.

4. **Identify your "phone-a-friend."** Find one senior SE who is willing to answer your questions without judgement. This is not your manager — it's a peer who remembers what it felt like to be new. Ask them: "If you could go back to your first month, what would you wish someone told you?" Then actually listen to the answer.

5. **Set up your demo environment.** Before you shadow a single call, get your demo instance running, configured, and loaded with sample data. Nothing is worse than getting asked to "jump in and show them the workflow" on day 10 and having nothing to show. Even if it's rough, having a working environment builds your confidence faster than studying documentation.

> **War Story:** A new SE spent his entire first week reading product documentation — 400 pages of technical specs, API references, and release notes. By day 5, he could answer any trivia question about the platform. On day 8, he shadowed a discovery call. The prospect asked, "How would you solve our data reconciliation problem between SAP and Snowflake?" He had no idea. He knew every feature but had never mapped a feature to a real business problem. He'd studied the menu instead of learning to cook.

> **Failure Mode:** The new SE who tries to "prove themselves" by volunteering to lead a demo in week one. They don't know the product well enough to recover from an unexpected question. They don't know the prospect's context well enough to tell a relevant story. The demo goes poorly, the AE loses confidence, and the SE spends the next month trying to rebuild trust internally. First impressions with AEs are hard to reverse.

**Interview Angle:**
"Describe how you onboarded into your last presales role. What did you prioritise in the first two weeks?"
A strong answer shows intentional orientation — product learning, deal history review, relationship building — without rushing to perform. A weak answer says "I just jumped into deals immediately."

**Practical Reference: The Week 1–2 Orientation Checklist**

- [ ] Identified the top 5 use cases that close deals
- [ ] Read 10 closed-won deal histories in CRM
- [ ] Met 1 person from Product, Engineering, PS, CS, Marketing
- [ ] Found a "phone-a-friend" senior SE
- [ ] Demo environment running with sample data
- [ ] Shadowed at least 2 customer calls (observation only)
- [ ] Documented 5 questions to ask my manager at our first 1:1

🟢 **Beginner** — Orientation is about building mental models, not memorising facts. The SE who understands *why* customers buy will always outperform the SE who only knows *what* the product does.

## 0.3.2 Week 3-6: Shadow Before You Lead

By week three, the temptation to "do real work" becomes overwhelming. The AEs are sending you calendar invites. Your manager is asking when you'll be "ramped." Your ego is telling you that you've seen enough to fly solo. Resist it — at least partially. Weeks 3–6 are your apprenticeship phase. You shadow with intent, debrief with rigour, and make your first controlled contributions.

1. **Shadow with a specific focus per call.** Don't just sit on calls passively. Before each shadowed meeting, pick one thing to observe. Monday: watch how the senior SE opens a discovery call. Wednesday: watch how they handle an objection about missing features. Friday: watch how they transition from the demo to next steps. Passive observation teaches nothing. Targeted observation teaches everything.

2. **Run the "invisible debrief."** After every shadowed call, spend 10 minutes writing down: What did the SE do well? What would I have done differently? What question did the prospect ask that I couldn't have answered? Share your debrief notes with the senior SE and ask for their reaction. This single practice will accelerate your ramp by 40% because it forces you to engage analytically with what you observed, not just absorb it passively.

3. **Take your first "section" of a call.** Around week 4, ask the senior SE if you can run one specific section of a customer call — typically the product overview or a single use-case walkthrough. Not the full demo. Not discovery. One contained, rehearsed segment that you've practised three times internally. This is your controlled exposure. If it goes well, you build confidence. If it stumbles, the senior SE is there to recover.

4. **Build your first persona-mapped demo narrative.** Take one of the top 5 use cases and build a 15-minute demo script around a specific persona. Not "here's how feature X works." Instead: "You're a data engineering manager at a mid-market fintech. Your team spends 3 hours every morning manually reconciling yesterday's transactions. Here's how that changes." Personas make features stick. Practice this narrative until you can deliver it without notes.

5. **Start logging your "I don't know" list.** Every question you encounter that you can't answer goes on a list. Not to memorise the answer — to understand *the pattern* of questions that matter. After 4 weeks, your list will cluster around 3–4 themes: integration, security, scalability, and pricing. Those clusters become your study priorities for weeks 6–10.

> **War Story:** A new SE shadowed a senior colleague on a complex discovery call. The prospect's lead architect asked about database failover behaviour during a regional outage. The senior SE answered confidently and moved on. In the debrief, the new SE said, "I noticed you didn't mention that our failover takes 90 seconds, not the 30 seconds you implied." The senior SE paused and said, "You're right. I rounded down because the architect's concern was about data loss, not speed. If I'd said 90 seconds, he would have fixated on the number instead of hearing that zero data is lost." The new SE learned more about presales communication from that single exchange than from any training module.

> **Failure Mode:** The new SE who shadows for 6 weeks and never debriefs. They sit on 30 calls, absorb 30% of what happened, and when they're finally asked to lead a meeting, they replicate the senior SE's style but not the substance. They mimic the tonality without understanding the decision-making underneath. They sound confident until the first unexpected question, at which point they freeze because they never processed *why* the senior SE said what they said.

**Interview Angle:**
"Tell me about a time you learned a critical skill by observing another SE. What did you observe and what did you take from it?"
A strong answer identifies a specific behaviour or decision, explains the reasoning behind it, and describes how it changed their own approach. A weak answer says "I learned a lot from watching senior people."

**Practical Reference: The Shadow Call Debrief Template**

| Call Date | Prospect | Senior SE | My Focus Area |
|-----------|----------|-----------|---------------|
| | | | e.g., "how they handled the pricing question" |

**Post-Call Notes:**
- What did the SE do well?
- What would I have done differently?
- What question came up that I couldn't answer?
- Key takeaway for my own practice:

🟢 **Beginner** — Shadowing without debriefing is just sitting in meetings. The debrief is where the learning happens. Make it a non-negotiable habit in this phase.

## 0.3.3 Week 6-10: Lead With a Safety Net

You've oriented. You've shadowed. You've absorbed the patterns. Weeks 6–10 are where you take the wheel — but with someone riding shotgun who can grab it if you're about to drive off a cliff. This is the "supervised solo" phase, and it's the most important period in your entire ramp because the habits you form here will define your performance for the next 18 months.

1. **Lead your first discovery call with a senior SE on mute.** Book a discovery meeting on a deal your SE manager considers low-risk. Prepare your discovery questions using the frameworks you've observed. Run the call. Your senior SE sits on the call with camera off and mic muted — available to jump in only if you hit a wall you genuinely cannot navigate. After the call, debrief immediately. Not in 2 hours. Not tomorrow. Immediately, while every micro-decision is still fresh.

2. **Run your first solo demo — but rehearse it three times first.** No SE should deliver a customer demo they haven't rehearsed at least three times internally. Record your rehearsal. Watch it back. Time it. Identify the two weakest transitions and fix them. Your first live demo will be roughly 60% as good as your best rehearsal. That's normal. The rehearsals raise the floor so that 60% is still good enough.

3. **Make your first mistake — and own it publicly.** You will say something wrong in a customer meeting during this phase. You will misquote a spec, overclaim a capability, or fumble a question. When it happens, correct it in writing within 24 hours: "Following up from our call — I want to clarify that the batch processing limit is 500K rows, not 1M as I mentioned. Here's the documentation." This builds more credibility than getting it right in the first place. Customers don't expect perfection. They expect honesty.

4. **Start managing your own deal notes independently.** Stop relying on the AE's summary of what happened. After every call you lead, write your own technical notes in the CRM within 1 hour. Include: architecture discussed, requirements surfaced, unknown items, and agreed next steps. When the AE reads your notes and says, "I didn't catch that detail about their middleware constraint," you've proven your value.

5. **Ask for structured feedback from your AE partners.** After each customer-facing interaction, send a one-line Slack message to the AE: "Quick feedback — what's the one thing I should do differently next time?" This does two things: it signals that you take improvement seriously, and it gives the AE permission to coach you without feeling awkward. AEs who trust their SE's commitment to growth will give them better deals.

> **War Story:** A new SE led her first discovery call. She had prepared 12 questions. She asked the first question, got a detailed 4-minute answer, and then moved straight to question 2 without acknowledging anything the prospect had said. By question 5, the prospect was giving one-word answers. The senior SE messaged her privately: "Echo back what they said before your next question." She started paraphrasing: "So if I understand correctly, the core issue is that your ETL pipeline breaks every time the schema changes — is that right?" The prospect's entire demeanour shifted. "Yes, exactly." The rest of the call was the best discovery conversation the senior SE had heard in months. One coaching note. One real-time adjustment. One completely different outcome.

> **Failure Mode:** The new SE who avoids customer-facing work because they "don't feel ready yet." They stay in the shadowing phase for 3 months, reading documentation and building demo environments nobody asked for. Their manager mistakes quietness for thoroughness. When they finally lead a call, they're paralysed by the gap between what they've studied and what actually happens in a live meeting. The longer you delay leading, the harder the transition becomes. Imperfect action beats perfect preparation.

**Interview Angle:**
"Describe a mistake you made early in your presales career and how you handled it."
A strong answer demonstrates transparency, a specific correction made with the customer, and a process change adopted as a result. A weak answer minimises the mistake or blames circumstances.

**Practical Reference: The First Solo Meeting Readiness Checklist**

- [ ] Discovery questions prepared and rehearsed
- [ ] Demo environment tested and working
- [ ] Pre-meeting CRM review completed
- [ ] Senior SE confirmed as backup on the call
- [ ] AE aligned on meeting objectives and roles
- [ ] Immediate post-call debrief scheduled (15 minutes)
- [ ] CRM technical notes logged within 1 hour

🟢 **Beginner** — The goal of this phase is not perfection. It's *functional independence with a feedback loop*. You should be able to lead a standard discovery and demo by week 10 — imperfectly, but independently.

## 0.3.4 Week 10-90: Build Your Personal Operating System

By week 10, you're no longer the new SE. You're a functioning member of the team. You're running discovery calls, delivering demos, and logging CRM notes. The safety net is mostly gone. What separates SEs who plateau at "functional" from those who accelerate to "exceptional" is what happens in weeks 10–90: the construction of a personal operating system.

A personal operating system is the set of habits, templates, and rituals that make your presales execution repeatable, scalable, and improvable. Without one, every deal is a fresh start. With one, every deal builds on the last.

1. **Build your personal demo library.** Record yourself delivering each of your top 5 use-case demos. Watch them back quarterly. Identify what's getting sharper and what still needs work. Store your best demo recordings as benchmarks — when you have a rough week, watching yourself at your best recalibrates your confidence.

2. **Create your discovery question bank.** After 10+ discovery calls, you'll notice patterns. Certain questions consistently unlock valuable information. Others consistently produce vague or defensive answers. Curate the winners. Organise them by persona (engineer vs. VP vs. CFO) and by topic (architecture, pain, budget, timeline). This bank becomes your pre-call preparation accelerator.

3. **Establish your weekly deal review rhythm.** Every Monday morning, spend 30 minutes reviewing your active deals. For each deal, answer: What's the next step? What's the biggest risk? What have I not done yet? This prevents deals from going dark because you got distracted by a flashier opportunity. The worst feeling in presales is opening your pipeline on Friday and realising you haven't touched a deal in 3 weeks.

4. **Start an objection log.** Every time a prospect raises an objection you haven't heard before, write it down along with how you handled it (or failed to handle it). Over time, this log becomes a personal playbook of battle-tested responses. After 6 months, you'll have 30–50 entries. After a year, you'll rarely encounter an objection you haven't already thought through.

5. **Track your personal metrics.** Don't wait for your manager to tell you your win rate. Calculate it yourself monthly. How many demos did I deliver? How many progressed to POC? How many POCs resulted in technical wins? If your demo-to-POC conversion is 60% but your POC-to-win rate is only 30%, you have a POC execution problem, not a demo problem. Self-awareness drives self-improvement.

> **War Story:** An SE built a ruthlessly simple operating system in her first 90 days: a Monday deal review, a Friday objection log update, and a monthly metric self-assessment. Her manager noticed that her win rate climbed from 22% in Q1 to 38% in Q2 — not because she got dramatically better at demoing, but because she started declining poorly qualified deals (discovery question bank revealed weak signals) and investing her POC energy in deals with confirmed champions (deal review rhythm surfaced single-threaded risks early). She was promoted to Senior SE within 14 months. The operating system was the differentiator.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who treats every deal as a unique event with no connection to the last one. They never capture patterns, never review their objection handling, never track their own metrics. After 12 months, they've "done 80 demos" but have the pattern recognition of someone who's done 8 — because they never processed the experiences into reusable knowledge. They're busy. They're not improving.

**Interview Angle:**
"How do you track and improve your own performance as an SE?"
A strong answer describes a specific, self-directed system — metrics tracking, objection logs, demo recording reviews, or weekly deal reviews. A weak answer says "my manager tracks our team metrics in the CRM."

**Practical Reference: The SE Personal Operating System Template**

| Ritual | Frequency | Time | Purpose |
|--------|-----------|------|---------|
| Deal portfolio review | Weekly (Monday) | 30 min | Identify at-risk deals, prioritise actions |
| Objection log update | Weekly (Friday) | 15 min | Capture and process new objections |
| Demo self-recording review | Monthly | 45 min | Benchmark personal demo quality |
| Metric self-assessment | Monthly | 20 min | Track demo-to-POC, POC-to-win, cycle length |
| Discovery question bank refresh | Quarterly | 30 min | Add winners, retire weak questions |

Total weekly time investment: ~1.5 hours. ROI: career-defining.

🟢 **Beginner** — The operating system doesn't have to be perfect. It has to exist. A rough system you follow consistently beats a beautiful system you abandon after two weeks.
