# 0.1 What Even Is Presales?

## 0.1.1 Presales in Plain English

Most people stumble into presales because they were too technical for sales and too commercial for engineering. Nobody grows up dreaming about running product demos for a living. And yet, the role of the Solutions Engineer sits at the single most influential intersection in any technology company — the exact point where a prospect decides whether your product is worth millions of dollars or a polite "we'll circle back."

Here's what presales actually is, stripped of the corporate fluff:

1. **You are the translator.** Your company builds technology. Your prospect has a business problem. The Account Executive (AE) promises the prospect your technology solves that problem. Your job is to *prove it* — technically, credibly, and under pressure. You're the person in the room who has to answer "but does it actually work with our Oracle 11g instance running on-prem in Frankfurt?"

2. **You are not a demo jockey.** The most damaging misconception in the industry is that SEs exist to click through product screens. If all you do is give tours of the UI, you will be automated out of a job within five years. Your value is in *diagnosis* — understanding the prospect's real problem (which they often can't articulate themselves) and architecting a credible path to solving it.

3. **You own the "technical win."** In enterprise sales, there are two wins required to close a deal. The *commercial win* belongs to the AE — negotiating terms, navigating procurement, getting signatures. The *technical win* belongs to you — convincing the prospect's technical team that your solution works, integrates, scales, and won't blow up at 3 AM on a Friday. Without the technical win, the commercial win doesn't happen.

4. **You influence revenue without carrying a bag.** Most SEs don't carry a direct quota (though some do). But every dollar of closed revenue touched by a presales engagement is SE-influenced revenue. In high-performing organisations, an SE typically influences $3–8M in annual pipeline. That makes you one of the highest-leverage individual contributors in the company — if you can prove it.

5. **You sit in the uncomfortable middle.** Sales thinks you're too slow and too cautious. Engineering thinks you're too commercial and too willing to promise features that don't exist. Product thinks you're a distraction from the roadmap. Your job is to navigate all of that friction and still make the prospect feel like they're talking to the smartest, most trustworthy person in the room.

> **War Story:** I watched a new SE spend her entire first quarter memorising every feature in the product catalogue. She could answer any question about any module instantly. She lost every deal she touched. Why? Because she answered questions nobody asked. She'd volunteer technical detail that frightened prospects instead of reassuring them. The moment she learned to *listen first and speak second*, her win rate doubled in a single quarter. Knowing the product inside out is table stakes. Knowing when to deploy that knowledge — that's the actual skill.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who defines their job as "I support the AE." This mental model is career poison. It positions you as subordinate, reactive, and expendable. When layoffs come, "support" roles get cut first. When promotions open, "support" people don't get considered. You don't support deals — you *shape* them. The distinction matters.

**Interview Angle:**
"How would you explain what a Solutions Engineer does to someone outside the tech industry?"
A strong answer avoids product-specific language and focuses on the translation role — bridging technical capability and business value. A weak answer describes demo mechanics.

**Practical Reference: The 60-Second SE Elevator Pitch**
Use this when anyone — a prospect, a recruiter, your family — asks what you do:
*"I help companies evaluate whether our technology solves their specific business problem. I run the technical side of the sales process — discovery, architecture, proof of concepts — so the customer can make a confident decision. If the deal closes, it's because I proved it works. If it doesn't, it's usually because I surfaced a legitimate gap before the company wasted time and money."*

🟢 **Beginner** — At this level, simply understanding that presales is not "sales plus coding" is the critical mindset shift. Everything else builds from here.

## 0.1.2 The Cast of Characters

Walk into any enterprise deal and you'll find the same cast of characters on both sides of the table. The problem is, nobody gives you a programme when you join. You're expected to figure out who matters, who's dangerous, and who's just filling a seat — usually while the meeting is already underway.

Here's your cast list. Learn it before your first call.

**Your Side of the Table:**

1. **The Account Executive (AE)** — Owns the commercial relationship. Responsible for pipeline generation, deal progression, and contract negotiation. The AE's primary metric is closed revenue. They are your closest partner and, occasionally, your most dangerous liability. A great AE clears the path so you can run. A bad AE blocks the path and expects you to bulldoze through it anyway.

2. **The Solutions Engineer (SE) — That's You** — Owns the technical win. Responsible for discovery, solution design, demonstrations, proof of concepts, and technical credibility. You are the person the prospect's engineers trust (or don't). Everything covered in this playbook is your domain.

3. **The SE Manager / Director of Solutions** — Your direct leadership. Sets coverage models, manages SE-to-AE ratios, tracks technical win rates, and advocates for headcount. In healthy organisations, this person shields you from low-quality deals and fights for your time. In dysfunctional ones, they just forward AE requests to your calendar.

4. **The Sales Development Representative (SDR/BDR)** — Generates initial meetings through outbound prospecting. By the time a deal reaches you, the SDR has typically qualified it at a surface level. Their qualification is often shallow — they confirmed the prospect is willing to take a meeting, not that the prospect has budget, authority, or a real problem. Expect to re-qualify.

5. **Professional Services / Implementation** — The team that builds what you sold. Your solution design becomes their implementation spec. If you over-promise during the sales cycle, these are the people who suffer. Build a relationship with your PS lead early — they'll tell you what's actually deployable and what's a fantasy.

**The Prospect's Side of the Table:**

1. **The Champion** — The internal advocate who wants your solution to win. They're usually a mid-to-senior manager who has a personal stake in solving the problem you address. Your champion will give you insider information, coach you on internal politics, and fight for your solution in rooms you'll never enter. Lose your champion, lose the deal.

2. **The Economic Buyer** — The person who can sign the cheque. Often a VP, SVP, or CxO. They care about business outcomes, risk, and ROI — not your API throughput. You may never meet them directly. When you do, you get 15 minutes, not 60. Make every sentence count.

3. **The Technical Evaluator** — A senior engineer, architect, or IT director tasked with validating your solution technically. They will test your claims. They will find your gaps. They are not your enemy — they are your credibility checkpoint. Win their respect and they become your strongest advocate. Lose it and no amount of AE charm will save the deal.

4. **The Detractor** — Someone on the prospect's side who does not want you to win. Maybe they built the legacy system you're replacing. Maybe they prefer a competitor. Maybe they're protecting their team's headcount. Detractors operate quietly — they raise concerns in meetings you're not invited to, they add requirements designed to disqualify you, they slow procurement to a crawl. You must identify them early and decide whether to convert, neutralise, or navigate around them.

5. **Procurement** — The team responsible for vendor evaluation, contract negotiation, and compliance. They are not your enemy, but they are not your friend either. Their job is to reduce your price and increase your legal exposure. Treat them with respect and prepare for their process — it's the single biggest cause of deal delays in enterprise sales.

> **War Story:** An SE joined a deal three months in. Nobody briefed them on the cast. In the first technical deep-dive, they treated the "quiet person in the corner" as a junior attendee and directed all answers to the loud engineering manager. The quiet person was the CTO. The CTO decided in that meeting that the SE lacked judgement. The deal closed — with a competitor. Always ask the AE before every meeting: *who is in the room and what do they care about?*

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who builds a relationship exclusively with the champion and ignores the economic buyer. The champion gets promoted, moves to a different department, or leaves the company. The deal has no executive sponsorship. It stalls and dies in procurement. You invested four months of technical work for nothing.

**Interview Angle:**
"Tell me about a deal where you had to navigate a hostile stakeholder. How did you identify them, and what did you do?"
A strong answer demonstrates early detection, a deliberate engagement strategy, and an outcome tied to the deal result. A weak answer describes getting blindsided or "winning them over with a great demo."

**Practical Reference: The Pre-Meeting Cast Sheet**
Before every customer-facing meeting, fill out this 4-row sheet:

| Name | Title | Role in Deal | What They Care About | Risk Level |
|------|-------|-------------|---------------------|------------|
|      |       | Champion / Evaluator / Buyer / Detractor / Unknown | Business outcome / Technical validation / Political cover / Cost reduction |  Low / Med / High |

Share this with your AE before the meeting. Update it after every interaction.

🟢 **Beginner** — At this level, simply knowing the roles exist is the win. As you progress, you'll learn to read the room in real time and adapt your messaging per persona without preparation.

## 0.1.3 The Presales Glossary

Your first week in presales will feel like everyone around you is speaking a language they assume you already know. Nobody stops to define terms. The AE will say "we need to MEDDPICC this deal" and expect you to nod. The SE manager will ask about your "technical win rate" and expect a number. Your champion will reference "the RFP we're responding to" and assume you've read it.

This glossary is your survival guide. Bookmark it. Refer to it without shame. Every senior SE you admire had a moment when they Googled "what does ARR mean" — they just won't admit it.

**Deal & Pipeline Terms:**

1. **ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)** — The annualised value of a subscription contract. A $120K/year SaaS deal = $120K ARR. This is how your company measures growth and how your AE measures success.

2. **ACV (Annual Contract Value)** — Similar to ARR but includes one-time fees like implementation. A $120K/year subscription + $30K implementation = $150K ACV in year one.

3. **Pipeline** — The total value of all deals currently being worked. An AE might say "I have $4M in pipeline" — meaning $4M worth of deals in various stages of progression. Your pipeline is a leading indicator: too thin and you'll miss quota, too bloated and you're wasting time on deals that won't close.

4. **Technical Win** — The moment the prospect's technical team confirms your solution meets their requirements. This is your primary deliverable as an SE. It typically happens after a successful POC or a particularly strong discovery-into-demo sequence.

5. **Closed-Won / Closed-Lost** — The final disposition of a deal. Closed-Won means the contract was signed. Closed-Lost means the prospect chose a competitor, chose no solution, or the deal died for any reason. Both outcomes are valuable learning opportunities.

**Methodology & Qualification Terms:**

1. **MEDDPICC** — Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Process, Decision Criteria, Paper Process, Identified Pain, Champion, Competition. The most widely used enterprise sales qualification framework. As an SE, you'll primarily contribute to Metrics (quantifying pain), Decision Criteria (shaping evaluation), Identified Pain (surfacing it in discovery), and Competition (understanding who you're up against).

2. **BANT** — Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline. An older, simpler qualification framework. Still useful for quick gut-checks: does this prospect have money, can they decide, do they need what we sell, and when?

3. **Discovery** — The diagnostic conversation where you uncover the prospect's current pain, existing architecture, business impact, and requirements. This is arguably the single most important skill in presales. Bad discovery leads to bad demos, bad POCs, and lost deals.

4. **Champion** — An internal advocate at the prospect's organisation who actively wants your solution to win and is willing to spend political capital to make it happen. A deal without a champion is a deal without a pulse.

5. **Multi-threading** — Building relationships with multiple stakeholders at the prospect to reduce single-point-of-failure risk. If your only contact leaves, gets promoted, or goes dark, a multi-threaded deal survives. A single-threaded deal dies.

**Technical Presales Terms:**

1. **POC (Proof of Concept)** — A structured technical evaluation where the prospect tests your product against agreed success criteria. A POC is your highest-investment, highest-risk presales activity. It typically takes 2–6 weeks and consumes significant SE bandwidth.

2. **RFP (Request for Proposal)** — A formal document issued by a prospect inviting vendors to submit a detailed proposal. RFPs range from 20 questions to 2,000 questions. Not all are worth responding to — and knowing which to decline is a critical skill.

3. **Harbor Tour** — A product demo that walks through every feature in sequence, like a guided tour of a harbor. This is the worst possible demo format. It bores the audience, fails to connect to their specific pain, and hands the initiative to whoever asks the first tangential question.

4. **Technical Debt** — Existing architectural shortcuts or legacy decisions in the prospect's environment that will complicate your integration. Discovering technical debt early saves you from building a POC on quicksand.

5. **As-Is / To-Be Architecture** — The prospect's current state (as-is) versus the proposed future state with your solution (to-be). Your solution design is essentially the bridge between these two diagrams.

> **War Story:** An SE joined a discovery call and heard the AE say "we need to get to paper by end of quarter." The SE didn't know what "get to paper" meant (it means getting a contract in front of the legal team for signature). They spent the next two weeks preparing a detailed technical document instead of helping accelerate the procurement process. The deal slipped a quarter. One undefined term. Six figures of delayed revenue.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who nods along when they don't understand a term because they're afraid of looking junior. This compounds day after day until they're operating on assumptions that diverge further and further from reality. Six weeks in, they discover they've been building a POC against requirements that were actually "nice-to-haves" the prospect mentioned once in passing — because they didn't understand what "MoSCoW prioritisation" meant when the evaluator referenced it.

**Interview Angle:**
"What does a 'technical win' mean to you, and how do you know when you've achieved one?"
A strong answer describes a formal or semi-formal declaration from the prospect's technical team, ideally documented and shared with the economic buyer. A weak answer says "when the demo goes well."

**Practical Reference: The Week-One Cheat Card**
Print this. Keep it on your desk for the first 30 days:

| Acronym | Stands For | Why You Care |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| ARR | Annual Recurring Revenue | How your company measures growth |
| ACV | Annual Contract Value | How individual deals are sized |
| POC | Proof of Concept | Your highest-stakes deliverable |
| RFP | Request for Proposal | Formal evaluations you'll respond to |
| MEDDPICC | Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Process... | The deal qualification framework your AE uses |
| TCO | Total Cost of Ownership | How prospects compare your price to alternatives |
| ROI | Return on Investment | How you justify the spend to the CFO |

🟢 **Beginner** — At this level, survival means not getting lost in the jargon. Within 90 days, these terms will be second nature and you'll start using them to *shape* conversations rather than just follow them.

## 0.1.4 How Presales Differs From Engineering, Support & Consulting

If you came from software engineering, your instinct is to build the perfect solution. If you came from customer support, your instinct is to fix the immediate problem. If you came from consulting, your instinct is to scope a 6-month engagement with a 40-page statement of work. All three instincts will get you into trouble in presales.

Presales occupies a unique space. You need enough technical depth to be credible, enough commercial awareness to be strategic, and enough emotional intelligence to read a room full of people who aren't telling you what they actually think. No other function in a technology company demands all three simultaneously, under time pressure, with millions of dollars on the line.

Here's how presales compares to the roles you might be coming from:

1. **Engineering builds. Presales proves.** An engineer's job ends when the code is deployed and the tests pass. An SE's job ends when a sceptical architect says, "Okay, I believe this will work in our environment." Engineers have days or weeks to iterate. You have 60 minutes in a demo to earn credibility or lose it permanently. An engineer can ship a fix. You can't un-say a claim you made on a recorded call.

2. **Support reacts. Presales anticipates.** A support engineer receives a ticket describing a problem that already exists. An SE receives a calendar invite to a meeting where the problem hasn't been fully articulated yet — and might not be the real problem at all. Support solves known issues. Presales diagnoses unknown ones. The diagnostic skill (knowing which questions to ask before anyone asks you anything) is the fundamental presales muscle.

3. **Consulting scopes. Presales sells.** A consultant arrives after the contract is signed and has weeks to ramp up. An SE arrives before the contract exists and has one meeting to demonstrate value. Consultants scope projects for thoroughness. SEs scope solutions for winnability — the minimum viable architecture that proves the concept and earns the technical win, not the 18-month digital transformation plan that's technically superior but impossible to sell into a Q4 budget freeze.

4. **Engineering optimises for correctness. Presales optimises for conviction.** An engineer who says "it depends on the configuration parameters" is being accurate. An SE who says the same thing in front of a CFO is being useless. Your job is to provide enough precision to be credible and enough simplicity to be understood. The CFO doesn't need to know about your microservices architecture. They need to know that you'll eliminate 3 hours of manual reconciliation every day and save them $400K a year.

5. **Support is measured on resolution time. Presales is measured on deal outcomes.** If a support engineer resolves a ticket in 4 hours, they've done a great job regardless of the customer's revenue value. If an SE spends 4 weeks on a POC and the deal is lost, they've contributed nothing to the business — no matter how elegant their technical work was. Presales is a results business. The quality of your work is ultimately judged by whether the prospect signs the contract.

> **War Story:** A former platform engineer joined a presales team. In his first demo, a prospect asked about data replication latency. He spent 12 minutes explaining the internal message queue architecture, including partition strategies, consumer groups, and exactly how the acknowledgement protocol handled edge cases. The prospect's VP of Operations — who had asked the original question — picked up his phone and started reading emails. The AE had to physically interrupt to redirect the conversation. The engineer's answer was technically flawless. It was also the moment the prospect mentally disengaged from the evaluation. Three months of pipeline, gone in twelve minutes of unnecessary depth.

> **Failure Mode:** The former consultant who over-scopes every solution. They produce 30-page architecture documents for a Stage 2 deal. They propose 6-month implementation timelines when the competitor is proposing 6 weeks. They design the perfect solution instead of the winnable solution. Their technical work is consistently excellent and their close rate is consistently terrible. They keep blaming AEs for "not selling it properly" instead of recognising that the scope itself is what's killing the deals.

**Interview Angle:**
"How does your approach to problem-solving differ in presales compared to your previous engineering (or consulting or support) role?"
A strong answer demonstrates awareness of the *time compression*, the *commercial context*, and the shift from *building* to *proving*. A weak answer says "it's basically the same, I just present instead of code."

**Practical Reference: The Presales Translation Filter**
Before every customer interaction, run your planned content through this filter:

| If You're Thinking | Say This Instead | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "Let me explain how the architecture works" | "Let me show you why this matters for your team's daily workflow" | Prospects buy outcomes, not architecture |
| "We should scope a proper assessment first" | "Here's what we can prove in the next 2 weeks" | Speed wins deals. Thoroughness wins engagements — after the deal closes |
| "The answer is 'it depends'" | "In environments like yours, the typical answer is X. Let me validate the specifics afterward" | Confidence builds trust. Caveats build doubt |
| "I need to check with engineering" | "I'll confirm the exact spec by tomorrow. Based on similar deployments, I expect Y" | Show judgement now. Deliver precision later |

🟢 **Beginner** — Understanding what presales is *not* is as important as understanding what it is. The faster you shed your previous role's reflexes, the faster you'll develop the presales instinct: diagnose fast, prove credibly, close decisively.
