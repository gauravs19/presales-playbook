# Demonstration Strategy in Technical Presales

A presales demonstration is not a feature walkthrough or a product training session. It is a highly orchestrated performance designed to bridge the gap between technical capability and business value. A successful demo proves to the prospect that your solution solves their unique, documented pain points.

---

## üé≠ The "Tell, Show, Tell" Framework

The **Tell, Show, Tell** method ensures the prospect understands exactly what they are about to see, sees it clearly, and then digests the business value of what they just saw.

### 1. Tell (The Setup)
Before you click anything, set the stage. Frame the upcoming feature against a specific problem the prospect mentioned during discovery.
> *"Last week, Sarah mentioned that your engineers spend up to 4 hours manually piecing together trace logs during an outage. I‚Äôm going to show you how our unified observability dashboard automatically correlates those traces with infrastructure metrics in under 10 seconds."*

### 2. Show (The Execution)
Perform the action smoothly. Navigate intentionally. Avoid clicking around needlessly (what presales professionals call "the dance of the cursor"). Do not narrate every click; narrate the business flow.
> *(Clicks on the correlated incident view)* *"As you can see, the moment an alert fires, we automatically pull the exact trace, the failing database query, and the CPU spike into one single pane of glass."*

### 3. Tell (The Payoff)
Reiterate the value and confirm their understanding. Tie it explicitly to a business outcome (time saved, money saved, risk averted).
> *"By having this context instantly available, your Mean Time to Resolution (MTTR) drops significantly. Based on your current outage frequencies, this buys back roughly 15 engineering hours a week. How would your team utilize that reclaimed time?"*

## üö´ The "Harbor Tour" vs. The "Value Demo"

One of the most common pitfalls of junior presales engineers is giving the "Harbor Tour"‚Äîshowing every single feature of the product because it's interesting to them.

**The differences are stark:**

| **The Harbor Tour (Avoid)** | **The Value Demo (Strive For)** |
| -----------------------------| -------------------------------- |
| "Here is the settings menu where you configure users." | "To ensure SOC2 compliance, you can enforce SSO with Okta natively." |
| "Let me show you these 15 different dashboard widgets." | "Here is the singular widget that tracks the ROI metric the CFO cares about." |
| Scripted strictly based on the product. | Scripted stringently around the prospect's Discovery notes. |
| The SE does 90% of the talking. | The SE speaks 60%, leaving 40% for prospect interaction and confirmation. |

## üß© Structuring a Winning Demo Agenda

### 1. Executive Summary (5 Minutes)
Re-state the prospect's goals and pain points. Present the "Before" (their current, painful state) and the "After" (the desired state your solution provides). Secure agreement before opening the software.

### 2. The MacGuffin (2 Minutes)
Start with the absolute best, highest-value screen right away to hook their attention. Do not build up to it over 45 minutes; show the end result (e.g., the predictive analytics dashboard) immediately so they know where the journey leads.

### 3. The Value Flows (30-40 Minutes)
Break the demo into 2-3 specific workflows (Value Themes). Usually:
- **Theme 1:** Fixing their biggest operational pain.
- **Theme 2:** Empowering a key user persona.
- **Theme 3:** Satisfying the executive's reporting/visibility needs.

Ensure you pause for rigorous QA between flows. Read the room (or the Zoom screen) for non-verbal cues. If they look confused, pause and ask.

### 4. Review and Close (10 Minutes)
Summarize exactly what was proven today. Do not introduce new features. Ask the most critical closing question of the demo: 
> *"Based on what we've seen today, do you believe our platform can resolve [Pain Point X] and [Pain Point Y]?"*
Secure the technical win, then explicitly outline the next steps (e.g., moving to a POC, deep-dive architecture meeting, or commercial negotiations).

## üõ†Ô∏è Mitigating Demo Risks (The Demo Gremlins)

Even the best-planned demos can hit technical snags in SaaS environments.

- **Have a Backup Plan:** Pre-record offline video snippets of critical functionality. Keep them in a minimized folder on your desktop. If the internet dies or the staging server crashes, effortlessly pivot to the recording: *"It seems our demo environment is under heavy load right now, but let me show you exactly how this flow works via this capture."*
- **Clear the Clutter:** Hide your bookmarks bar. Turn off Slack/Teams notifications (Do Not Disturb). Close all irrelevant browser tabs. Ensure your desktop background is professional.
- **Never Fake It:** If the software throws an error, don't pretend it didn't happen. Address it calmly. *"Ah, it looks like a configuration error in my sandbox setup for the integration, but the core mechanism remains identical. Let‚Äôs move forward and I will send you a quick Loom video of the seamless integration right after this call."*
