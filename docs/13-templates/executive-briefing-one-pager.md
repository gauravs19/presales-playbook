#### 13.7.1 Executive Briefing One-Pager

<div class="template-meta">
  <span class="stage-badge st-close">Stage 07 · Close</span>
</div>

Executives decide whether a meeting matters before it starts — based on the pre-read. This one-pager is that pre-read: it frames the decision, not the product, and earns you a room where the conversation starts at altitude instead of climbing to it. It operationalises [Running an Executive Briefing](docs/09-executive-selling/running-executive-briefings.md), [Translating Technical Risk Into Business Risk](docs/09-executive-selling/technical-risk-as-business-risk.md), and [The One Diagram Every Executive Presentation Needs](docs/04-solution-design/one-diagram-executives-need.md).

| | |
|---|---|
| **When to use** | 48 hours before any CxO meeting — briefing, steering review, or final pitch |
| **Owner** | SE, pressure-tested by AE and champion |
| **Time to complete** | ~20 minutes (assuming the business case exists) |
| **Output goes to** | Executive attendees via the champion; doubles as your talk track |

---

## The Template

```markdown
# Executive Briefing — [Account] × [Vendor] · [Date] · 30 minutes

## Why This Meeting (two sentences)
[Account] is deciding how to address [problem], which currently costs [number] per [period]
and blocks [strategic initiative the EXEC owns]. This briefing presents the validated
option and the decision needed to hit [their date/fiscal event].

## What Your Team Found (their evidence, not our claims)
- Evaluation ran [dates] against 3 criteria your team defined. **Result: [3/3] met.**
- [Evaluation lead name]: "[verbatim verdict quote]"
- Business case (attached): payback in [X] months, conservative case.

## The Decision Structure
| Option | What it means | 3-yr cost | Risk |
|--------|---------------|-----------|------|
| 1. Do nothing | [problem] continues at [cost/period] | $[cumulative] | Compounds with scale |
| 2. [Realistic alternative — build / incumbent / defer] | | | [Honest assessment] |
| 3. Proposed: [solution, phased] | Phase 1 live in [X weeks] | $[TCO] | [Named risk + mitigation] |

## The One Diagram
[Before/after on one visual: today's bottleneck → target state. Zero logos, zero clip-art,
max 8 boxes. If it needs explaining, it isn't the diagram.]

## Risk We're Asking You to Weigh (candour, pre-empted)
The honest risk of Option 3 is [real risk]. We mitigate it by [specific mechanism —
phasing, contractual commitment, rollback path]. Your team reviewed this and [verdict].

## The Ask
Approve [specific decision] by [date] so that [phase 1 outcome] lands before [their event].
```

---

## How to Fill It In

1. **Anchor to the initiative the executive already owns.** "Reduces pipeline failures" is a manager's problem; "removes the scaling blocker on the data-platform initiative you committed to the board" is theirs. If you don't know their initiative, you're not ready for this meeting — go back to your champion.
2. **Three options, honestly priced.** A one-option pitch triggers sales-resistance; a three-option decision structure makes the executive the decider, which is the only role they'll accept. Option 2 must be genuinely viable — they'll know if it's a strawman, and your credibility dies with it.
3. **Name the risk before they do.** The pre-empted risk paragraph is what separates an advisor from a vendor. Executives don't expect zero risk; they expect to not be surprised.
4. **The ask has a date and a reason for the date.** "Approve by the 20th so phase 1 completes before your Q4 freeze" converts urgency from sales pressure into their own calendar math.

> **Failure Mode:** The fifteen-slide "executive deck" that spends eight slides on company history and product architecture before reaching a point. The executive checked out on slide 2 and delegated the decision back down to the team you already convinced — deal velocity dies in that delegation. One page, decision-first, and let the meeting be the conversation the page provokes.

🔵 **Senior** — Executives don't buy products; they retire risks and fund initiatives. Write the page in that language and the room is yours.
