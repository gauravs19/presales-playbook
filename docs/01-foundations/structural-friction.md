#### 1.2.1 Structural Friction & Quota Misalignment

The AE/SE relationship is the most important partnership in enterprise software â€” and the most structurally dysfunctional. Not because AEs are bad people or SEs are difficult. Because the incentive structures are fundamentally misaligned, and nobody designed them to be aligned. Understanding this friction is not about blame. It's about navigating a system that will never be fixed by complaining about it.

1. **The AE carries quota. You (usually) don't.** This single fact shapes every interaction. The AE's compensation, career trajectory, and job security are determined by a number â€” their quota attainment. When an AE asks you to "just do a quick demo for this deal," they're not being inconsiderate. They're operating under existential pressure to hit a target that determines whether they can pay their mortgage. Your job is to understand this pressure without being consumed by it.

2. **The AE needs breadth. You need depth.** An AE managing 30+ accounts wants every deal to progress. They want demos booked, proposals sent, and pipeline moving. You, managing 8â€“12 active deals, want to go deep on the ones that matter and qualify out the ones that don't. This creates daily friction: the AE sends you a calendar invite for a "quick discovery" on a deal that has no champion, no budget, and no timeline. You want to decline. The AE wants to keep the opportunity alive. Both of you are being rational. The system creates the conflict.

3. **Commit culture poisons the partnership.** In many sales organisations, AEs are required to "commit" a subset of their pipeline â€” promising that these deals will close this quarter. Once a deal is committed, it can't be un-committed without consequences. This means the AE needs your help whether the deal is real or wishful thinking. If you push back on a committed deal by saying "this doesn't feel real," you're not questioning the deal â€” you're threatening the AE's forecast. Tread carefully. Be right. And when you push back, bring data, not feelings.

4. **Territory and ownership create invisible walls.** AEs own accounts. SEs support accounts. When an SE builds a direct relationship with a champion or a technical evaluator, the AE can feel threatened â€” "Are they going around me?" â€” even if the SE is just doing their job effectively. The solution isn't to avoid building relationships. It's to be transparent: "Had a great conversation with their lead architect today. Here's what I learned and how it affects our strategy. Want to debrief for 10 minutes?" Keep the AE in the loop. Always.

5. **The compensation gap creates resentment if unmanaged.** In most companies, top-performing AEs earn significantly more than top-performing SEs â€” sometimes 2â€“3x. This creates a simmering resentment that poisons team dynamics if left unaddressed. The healthy response is to understand that AEs carry personal risk (miss quota, lose your job) that SEs typically don't. The unhealthy response is to withhold effort or undermine AE deals because "they get paid more for my work." If the compensation structure is genuinely unfair, negotiate or leave. Don't let bitterness degrade your professional performance.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who sees every AE request as an imposition. They resist demos, push back on meeting requests, and treat every deal qualification as a battle. They're technically right â€” many of those requests are low-value. But they're operationally destructive. AEs stop bringing them into deals. They get assigned to the worst accounts. "How do you manage the relationship with your AE partners, especially when you disagree on deal strategy?"
