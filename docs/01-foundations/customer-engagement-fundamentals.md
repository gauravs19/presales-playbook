# 1.5 Customer Engagement Fundamentals

## 1.5.1 Executive Presence

Executive presence is not charisma, confidence, or authority. It's the perception that you belong in the room — any room — because you add value that no one else in that room provides. SEs who have executive presence get invited to CxO meetings. SEs who don't get asked to "send the technical details after the call."

The bad news: executive presence can't be faked. The good news: it can be built, deliberately and incrementally.

1. **Start with silence.** The most common executive presence mistake is talking too much in high-stakes settings. When a CTO asks a hard question, the SE without presence starts answering immediately, verbose and rambling, trying to fill every second with words. The SE with presence pauses for 2–3 seconds, then delivers a concise answer. The pause accomplishes three things: it signals that you're thinking (not reacting), it creates gravity (silence is power), and it gives you time to choose the right framing.

2. **Speak in headlines, then unpack.** Executives process information top-down. Lead with the conclusion: "Yes, our platform can support your compliance requirements across all three jurisdictions." Then unpack the detail only if they ask. If they nod and move on, you've answered the question in 8 seconds instead of 8 minutes. If they probe deeper, you can layer in the specifics. Headline-first communication signals that you respect their time and understand their altitude.

3. **Own the room with your body, not your voice.** In person: sit upright, make deliberate eye contact, and occupy your full space at the table. Don't slouch, don't fidget, don't cross your arms. On video: camera at eye level, good lighting on your face, and an uncluttered background. These are not vanity considerations — they are visual trust signals. An executive meeting a vendor for the first time reads your physical presentation before they hear a single word.

4. **Use names deliberately.** When addressing a CTO, CFO, or any executive, use their name: "Sarah, the concern you raised about data sovereignty is one we see frequently in regulated industries." Using names creates personal connection, signals preparation, and transforms a generic product discussion into a directed conversation. It also prevents the executive from disengaging — hearing your own name activates attention.

5. **Admit what you don't know without apologising.** An SE without executive presence says: "Sorry, I don't — I'm not sure about that one, let me check..." An SE with presence says: "I want to give you a precise answer on that. I'll confirm the specific number and have it to you by end of day." Same outcome. Completely different impression. The first version signals uncertainty. The second signals discipline. Executives don't expect omniscience. They expect intellectual honesty delivered with composure.

> **War Story:** A Senior SE was invited to present to a FTSE 100 company's C-suite — CFO, CTO, and COO in the room. Her AE warned her: "The CFO interrupts everyone. Don't let it rattle you." Seven minutes into her presentation, the CFO cut in: "Skip the technology. Tell me why I should spend £2M with you instead of building it ourselves." She paused for 3 seconds. Then: "Because it would take your team 18 months to build what we deploy in 6 weeks — and during those 18 months, you'd miss two regulatory filing windows at £300K in penalties each. Our platform eliminates that risk from day one." Silence. The CFO nodded. "Continue." She didn't have a pre-prepared answer. She had practised the pause, the headline format, and the financial framing until they were reflexive. The deal closed at £2.4M.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who tries to build executive presence by being domineering. They talk over people, project artificial confidence, and use jargon to signal superiority. Executives see through this instantly — they deal with posturing daily. What they're pattern-matching for is substance and composure, not dominance. The SE who tries to command a room by force ends up commanding a room full of people who've decided not to buy.

**Interview Angle:**
"Tell me about a time you presented to C-level executives. How did you prepare and how did it go?"
A strong answer describes specific preparation (researching the executive's priorities, practising concise delivery), a real moment of pressure (a hard question, an interruption), and how they handled it. A weak answer describes the content of the presentation without commenting on the interpersonal dynamics.

**Practical Reference: The Executive Presence Checklist**
Before any C-suite meeting:

- [ ] Researched each executive's background, recent priorities, and public statements
- [ ] Prepared 3 "headline" answers to likely questions (conclusion first, detail on request)
- [ ] Practised the 3-second pause after hard questions
- [ ] Tested camera/audio/lighting (virtual) or confirmed seating and materials (in-person)
- [ ] Identified the #1 business outcome that matters most to the economic buyer
- [ ] Removed all jargon from my opening 5 minutes

🟡 **Mid-Level** — Executive presence is a muscle. It atrophies without practice and strengthens with repetition. Seek opportunities to present to senior audiences — even internal ones — at least once a month.

## 1.5.2 Virtual Body Language & Presence

In the post-2020 era, 80–90% of your presales interactions happen on Zoom, Teams, or Meet. The SE who mastered in-person presence but hasn't adapted to virtual is losing deals without knowing it — because virtual communication strips away 60% of the non-verbal signals that build trust in person. What remains is a tiny rectangle on a screen, and every pixel in that rectangle is either building credibility or eroding it.

1. **Camera position is status position.** A camera angled up at your face (laptop on desk) makes you look like you're looming over the audience. A camera angled down makes you look diminished. Eye-level camera — achieved with a laptop stand, external webcam on a monitor, or a stack of books — is the only acceptable setup. It creates a natural, peer-to-peer visual dynamic that communicates equality and confidence.

2. **Lighting determines trustworthiness.** This isn't a production note — it's neuroscience. Faces lit from the front are perceived as more trustworthy than faces lit from behind or from the side. A $30 ring light or a desk lamp positioned behind your monitor eliminates shadows, evens your skin tone, and ensures the prospect can read your facial expressions. A prospect who can't see your face clearly defaults to distrust — it's the visual equivalent of a weak handshake.

3. **Look at the camera, not the screen.** The most common virtual presence mistake: looking at the participant's video tile instead of the camera lens. On your screen, it feels like eye contact. On their screen, you're looking slightly downward — avoiding their gaze. When delivering key points, look directly at the camera lens for 3–5 seconds. This creates the illusion of direct eye contact and significantly increases perceived engagement and authority.

4. **Your background is your office.** A cluttered background communicates disorganisation. A virtual background communicates "I'm hiding something" (even if you're hiding laundry). The best background is a real, tidy space with 1–2 professional elements: a bookshelf, a clean wall, a plant. If you must use a virtual background, choose a static, simple image — not a beach, not a space station, not your company's branded template that screams "vendor."

5. **Manage the mute/unmute discipline.** Nothing destroys virtual presence faster than: keyboard sounds during someone else's talk, dogs barking during your key point, or the 3-second lag after someone asks you a question while you fumble for the unmute button. Stay muted unless actively speaking. When it's your turn, unmute smoothly — practise the physical motion of unmuting so there's no delay between the question and your response. That 2-second delay while you find the button communicates "I wasn't paying attention."

6. **Use screen sharing strategically.** When you share your screen, your face disappears from center stage. Use this deliberately: share when showing content, stop sharing when having a conversation. The transition between "content mode" and "conversation mode" should be conscious. Too many SEs share their screen at minute 1 and never stop — the entire meeting becomes a product walkthrough with no human connection.

> **War Story:** An SE was running a virtual demo for a prospect's executive team. His setup: laptop on a kitchen table, no external camera, no ring light, a window behind him creating silhouette, and his cat asleep on the chair next to him. The demo content was excellent. The product nailed every requirement. The prospect chose a competitor. In the debrief, the champion casually mentioned: "The other vendor's team felt more polished — they seemed more prepared for the meeting." "Polished" and "prepared" had nothing to do with technical content. It was entirely about visual presentation. The competing SE had a clean office, a ring light, and a camera at eye level. That $40 investment in lighting and positioning was the marginal difference in a $400K deal.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who dismisses virtual presence as "superficial." They focus on content quality ("my architecture diagram is superior") and ignore presentation quality ("I'm talking from a dark room with a messy background"). They're technically right that substance should matter more than style. They're practically wrong because decision-makers are human beings who process visual signals unconsciously. You can be the most technically competent SE in the industry and still lose to someone who looks more credible on camera.

**Interview Angle:**
"How do you ensure engagement during virtual customer meetings?"
A strong answer addresses both technical setup (camera, lighting, audio) and engagement techniques (camera use, screen share discipline, checking in with the audience). A weak answer focuses only on content delivery.

**Practical Reference: The Virtual Meeting Setup Scorecard**

| Element | Score: 1 (Poor) | Score: 5 (Professional) | My Current Score |
|---------|----------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Camera | Laptop angle, not eye level | External webcam at eye level | |
| Lighting | Backlit or dim | Front-lit, even, no shadows | |
| Background | Cluttered or virtual gimmick | Clean, real, professional | |
| Audio | Laptop mic, echo-prone | External mic or quality headset | |
| Eye contact | Looking at screen constantly | Camera-directed during key moments | |
| Screen share | On for entire meeting | Strategic, toggled between content and conversation | |

**If your total is below 20, invest 30 minutes and $50–$100 in your setup this week.** It will generate more ROI than any deal preparation you've done this quarter.

🟡 **Mid-Level** — Virtual presence is not optional. It's not secondary. In a world where 85% of your customer interactions happen on video, your virtual setup *is* your first impression.

## 1.5.3 Managing the Dominant or Hostile Stakeholder

In almost every complex deal, there's someone on the prospect's side who doesn't want you there. Maybe they built the legacy system you're replacing. Maybe they've already chosen a competitor. Maybe they resent being told by their management to evaluate external vendors when they'd rather build internally. Whatever the motivation, the hostile stakeholder will test you — publicly, aggressively, and often in the meeting where it matters most.

Your job isn't to win them over. It's to neutralise their ability to kill the deal while preserving the relationship for post-sale.

1. **Identify them before the meeting.** Ask the champion: "Is there anyone on the evaluation team who has concerns or reservations about this project?" Champions will usually tell you — often with relief, because they've been navigating the same personality internally. If the champion doesn't know, pay attention in the first 5 minutes of any group meeting: the hostile stakeholder is the one with crossed arms, delayed responses, or questions that start with "How do you handle..." (which is code for "I'm about to test you").

2. **Validate their expertise before addressing their concerns.** The most effective de-escalation technique is genuine acknowledgement: "That's a really sharp question — it tells me you've been through implementations like this before." This isn't flattery. It's recognition. Hostile stakeholders are often the most technically competent people in the room. Their hostility comes from a place of expertise — they've been burned by vendors before, and they're protecting their team from your promises. Recognising their expertise converts adversarial energy into collaborative energy.

3. **Answer directly. Never dodge.** The hostile stakeholder's favourite weapon is the gotcha question: "What happens to our data if we cancel the contract?" or "What's your uptime SLA, and what do you actually pay out when you miss it?" If you dodge, deflect, or give a vague answer, they'll escalate — and they'll do it in front of the economic buyer. Answer directly, even if the answer isn't what they want to hear: "Our standard SLA is 99.9% with a credit mechanism for downtime. I'll share the specific terms with you after the call." Direct answers disarm. Evasive answers arm.

4. **Give them a role in the evaluation.** Hostile stakeholders become less hostile when they have control. If their concern is security, make them the security evaluation lead: "Your InfoSec expertise is exactly what this evaluation needs. Would you be willing to lead the security review? I'll provide every document your team needs." This converts a detractor into a stakeholder with ownership. If they find a genuine gap, you'll know about it early (which is better than finding out in procurement). If they don't find a gap, their credibility shifts from opponent to endorser.

5. **Know when to stop engaging.** Some stakeholders cannot be converted. They've made their decision and they're using the evaluation process to build a case against you. When you recognise this — consistent hostility regardless of how well you answer, questions designed to waste your time rather than evaluate your product, active sabotage of the POC process — stop trying to win them. Instead, ensure that the other stakeholders (especially the champion and economic buyer) have enough positive evidence to move forward despite the opposition. You don't need unanimity. You need a majority.

> **War Story:** An SE walked into a discovery call with a financial services firm. The prospect's IT Director opened the meeting with: "I want to be upfront — I don't think we need an external platform. I've already spec'd a build using our existing Python stack." Instead of defending his product, the SE said: "That's a legitimate approach, and it might be the right one for your team. Can I ask a few questions about your build timeline and the resource allocation so I can give you an honest comparison?" The IT Director — who had expected a defensive pitch — was disarmed by the SE's willingness to validate the alternative. Over the next 45 minutes, the SE asked probing questions about the internal build that the IT Director hadn't fully thought through: maintenance burden, scaling challenges, opportunity cost of pulling engineers off product work. By the end of the call, the IT Director said: "I still prefer to build, but I'll admit you've raised points I need to think about." The deal didn't close that quarter. It closed the next — after the IT Director's internal build estimate ballooned from 3 months to 14 months, just as the SE had subtly implied.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who takes hostility personally. They match the stakeholder's energy — getting defensive, arguing technical points, or trying to "win" the exchange in front of the room. Every argument the SE wins in public is a relationship they lose in private. The hostile stakeholder now has personal motivation to kill the deal — not because the product is wrong, but because the SE embarrassed them. The prospect's internal dynamics become about saving face, not evaluating technology.

**Interview Angle:**
"Tell me about a stakeholder who actively opposed your solution. How did you handle them?"
A strong answer describes early identification, a deliberate engagement strategy (validation, role assignment, direct answers), and a pragmatic outcome (converted them, neutralised them, or proceeded despite them). A weak answer describes "winning the argument" or "avoiding them."

**Practical Reference: The Hostile Stakeholder Engagement Plan**

| Step | Action | Timing |
|------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Identify hostile stakeholders through champion pre-brief | Before first group meeting |
| 2 | Validate their expertise publicly in the meeting | First 10 minutes |
| 3 | Answer their questions directly, no evasion | During meeting |
| 4 | Offer them a role in the evaluation | End of first meeting |
| 5 | Follow up 1:1 with specific documentation addressing their concerns | Within 24 hours |
| 6 | Assess convertibility after 2 interactions | After second meeting |
| 7 | If not convertible, build stakeholder support around them | Ongoing |

🟡 **Mid-Level** — Hostile stakeholders are not obstacles. They're the deal's immune system. If your solution can survive their scrutiny, it has a stronger chance of surviving post-sale success — and they'll know it.

## 1.5.4 The Follow-Up That Feels Human

The post-meeting follow-up is the most underinvested interaction in presales. Most SEs treat it as an administrative task: paste bullet points into an email, attach a slide deck, send. Done. Next meeting. But the follow-up is where deals are actually advanced — because it's where you demonstrate listening, precision, and care in a format the prospect can share internally.

A follow-up that feels human makes the prospect feel heard. A follow-up that feels automated makes the prospect feel like a pipeline entry.

1. **Open with what you heard, not what you showed.** The first paragraph of every follow-up should summarise the prospect's stated pain — in their words, not yours. "Based on our conversation, your team is spending approximately 15 hours per week on manual data reconciliation between SAP and Snowflake, which is delaying your month-end close by 3–4 days." This accomplishes three things: it proves you listened, it documents the pain in writing (which the champion can forward internally), and it anchors the follow-up to the prospect's world rather than your product's world.

2. **Answer the questions you took offline.** If you said "I'll get back to you on that" during the meeting, this is where you deliver. Quickly. Precisely. With documentation. "You asked about our data residency options for EU customers. Our platform supports region-locked data processing in Frankfurt, Dublin, and London. Here's the documentation: [link]." Every offline question you answer in the follow-up builds trust. Every question you forget to answer erodes it.

3. **Name the next step explicitly — with a date.** "As discussed, we'd like to schedule a technical deep-dive with your infrastructure team to map integration requirements. Would Thursday, March 13th at 2 PM work for your group?" A vague "let's find time to continue the conversation" guarantees a 2-week scheduling delay. A specific date and time guarantees a yes/no response within 24 hours. The prospect's time is limited. Respect it by not making them do the scheduling work.

4. **Keep it under 300 words.** Your follow-up is competing with 47 other emails in the prospect's inbox. If it reads like a white paper, it won't be read. Bullet points. Short paragraphs. Bold the key points. The detail belongs in attachments, not in the email body. The email body is the executive summary that earns the click on the attachment.

5. **Personalise one element.** Include one line that references something specific, non-transactional from the meeting: "Enjoyed your comment about your team's experience migrating off the legacy Oracle system — that context is incredibly useful for shaping our approach." This single line transforms a vendor email into a human interaction. The prospect reads it and thinks, "They actually paid attention," instead of "They sent me a template."

> **War Story:** Two SEs were competing for the same deal. Both had successful demos. Both were technically strong. SE #1 sent a follow-up the next day: a 200-word email summarising the key pain points the prospect raised, three answers to questions taken offline (with links to docs), a proposed date for the next session, and one personal note about the prospect's question regarding their data centre migration timeline. SE #2 sent a follow-up 4 days later: a 600-word email recapping everything the product could do, with a 15-page slide deck attached. The champion forwarded SE #1's email to the CTO the same day with the note: "These are the people who understood what we need." The champion never forwarded SE #2's email. The deal closed with SE #1's company.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who sends "great meeting today, attaching the deck and a recording of the demo. Let me know if you have questions." This is not a follow-up. It's a file transfer. It communicates zero listening, zero personalisation, and zero initiative. The prospect's response will be no response — because the email doesn't ask for anything, contribute anything, or advance anything.

**Interview Angle:**
"What does your typical post-meeting follow-up process look like?"
A strong answer describes a structured, personalised follow-up that includes pain recapitulation, offline answers, and specific next steps. A weak answer describes "sending the deck and notes."

**Practical Reference: The Human Follow-Up Template**

```
Subject: Follow-up: [Meeting Topic] — [Specific Next Step]

Hi [Name],

Thank you for your time today. Here's a summary of what we discussed:

**Your Current Challenge:**
[2-3 sentences paraphrasing their stated pain in their language]

**Questions We Addressed Offline:**
- [Question 1]: [Answer + link to documentation]
- [Question 2]: [Answer + link to documentation]

**Next Step:**
[Specific proposed action] — would [specific date and time] work for your team?

[One personal/contextual note from the meeting]

Best,
[Your name]
```

Total writing time: 15 minutes. Impact: disproportionate.

🟡 **Mid-Level** — The follow-up is not the epilogue. It's the first page of the next chapter. Treat it with the same intentionality as the meeting itself.
