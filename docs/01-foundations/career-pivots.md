1. **Product Management.** SEs are the closest people in the company to the customer's actual problem. Product Managers who came from presales consistently outperform those from pure engineering backgrounds because they've heard hundreds of customers articulate their pain, and they know the difference between a feature request and a market signal. The transition: start by documenting field feedback systematically, attending product council meetings, and volunteering to co-author product briefs. 2. **Customer Success / Strategic Accounts.** If you love the customer relationship but hate the deal cycle uncertainty, Customer Success is a natural lateral move. CSMs who came from presales understand the technical architecture, can troubleshoot without escalating to support, and can identify expansion opportunities because they recognise the same buying signals they used to prospect for. The trade-off: lower variable compensation in most organisations, but higher relationship depth and lower weekly stress.

3. **Sales Leadership.** Some SEs transition to AE roles or sales management. This is the highest-risk pivot because it involves carrying quota â€” something most SEs have never done. The SEs who succeed as AEs are those who had strong Tier 3 and Tier 4 competencies: commercial acumen, organisational influence, and the ability to drive a deal from qualification to closed-won without relying on someone else to own the technical middle.

4. **Technical Marketing / Developer Relations.** If you're the SE who loves building demo environments, creating content, and presenting at conferences more than running individual deals, Technical Marketing or DevRel is a strong fit. You'll produce technical content (blogs, webinars, workshops) that generates demand â€” essentially doing presales at scale, 1-to-many instead of 1-to-1. The trade-off: less deal-level impact, more brand-level influence.

5. **Solutions Architecture (Post-Sales).** If you love the technical design work but hate the ambiguity of sales, Solutions Architecture in a post-sales or professional services context gives you deeper, longer-duration technical engagements without the uncertainty of whether the customer will buy.
