# 1.2 Presales vs. Sales — The Partnership Model

## 1.2.1 Structural Friction & Quota Misalignment

The AE/SE relationship is the most important partnership in enterprise software — and the most structurally dysfunctional. Not because AEs are bad people or SEs are difficult. Because the incentive structures are fundamentally misaligned, and nobody designed them to be aligned. Understanding this friction is not about blame. It's about navigating a system that will never be fixed by complaining about it.

1. **The AE carries quota. You (usually) don't.** This single fact shapes every interaction. The AE's compensation, career trajectory, and job security are determined by a number — their quota attainment. When an AE asks you to "just do a quick demo for this deal," they're not being inconsiderate. They're operating under existential pressure to hit a target that determines whether they can pay their mortgage. Your job is to understand this pressure without being consumed by it.

2. **The AE needs breadth. You need depth.** An AE managing 30+ accounts wants every deal to progress. They want demos booked, proposals sent, and pipeline moving. You, managing 8–12 active deals, want to go deep on the ones that matter and qualify out the ones that don't. This creates daily friction: the AE sends you a calendar invite for a "quick discovery" on a deal that has no champion, no budget, and no timeline. You want to decline. The AE wants to keep the opportunity alive. Both of you are being rational. The system creates the conflict.

3. **Commit culture poisons the partnership.** In many sales organisations, AEs are required to "commit" a subset of their pipeline — promising that these deals will close this quarter. Once a deal is committed, it can't be un-committed without consequences. This means the AE needs your help whether the deal is real or wishful thinking. If you push back on a committed deal by saying "this doesn't feel real," you're not questioning the deal — you're threatening the AE's forecast. Tread carefully. Be right. And when you push back, bring data, not feelings.

4. **Territory and ownership create invisible walls.** AEs own accounts. SEs support accounts. When an SE builds a direct relationship with a champion or a technical evaluator, the AE can feel threatened — "Are they going around me?" — even if the SE is just doing their job effectively. The solution isn't to avoid building relationships. It's to be transparent: "Had a great conversation with their lead architect today. Here's what I learned and how it affects our strategy. Want to debrief for 10 minutes?" Keep the AE in the loop. Always.

5. **The compensation gap creates resentment if unmanaged.** In most companies, top-performing AEs earn significantly more than top-performing SEs — sometimes 2–3x. This creates a simmering resentment that poisons team dynamics if left unaddressed. The healthy response is to understand that AEs carry personal risk (miss quota, lose your job) that SEs typically don't. The unhealthy response is to withhold effort or undermine AE deals because "they get paid more for my work." If the compensation structure is genuinely unfair, negotiate or leave. Don't let bitterness degrade your professional performance.

> **War Story:** An SE and AE were paired on a strategic account. The AE committed the deal for Q3 close. The SE knew the deal wasn't closing in Q3 — the prospect's procurement cycle was at least 12 weeks, and they were only in week 2. The SE said nothing because he didn't want to "create conflict." Q3 ended. The deal slipped. The AE missed quota by one deal. The VP of Sales asked what happened. The AE blamed the SE for "not flagging the timeline risk." The SE was blindsided. Lesson: silence is not diplomacy. If you see a risk, document it and communicate it early — even if the message is uncomfortable. "I want to flag that the timeline on this deal feels more like Q4 than Q3 based on where we are in procurement" protects you and helps the AE make better decisions.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who sees every AE request as an imposition. They resist demos, push back on meeting requests, and treat every deal qualification as a battle. They're technically right — many of those requests are low-value. But they're operationally destructive. AEs stop bringing them into deals. They get assigned to the worst accounts. Their pipeline shrinks. Their win rate drops — not because they can't do the work, but because nobody wants to work with them. Selectivity without partnership is isolation.

**Interview Angle:**
"How do you manage the relationship with your AE partners, especially when you disagree on deal strategy?"
A strong answer describes a specific mechanism (regular syncs, deal strategy alignment docs, transparent CRM notes) and a real example of productive disagreement. A weak answer says "we usually agree" or "I just do what the AE asks."

**Practical Reference: The AE/SE Partnership Charter**
Propose this to every new AE partner at the start of the relationship:

| Topic | Our Agreement |
|-------|--------------|
| Pre-meeting prep | AE shares context 24 hours before; SE reviews CRM before every call |
| Deal qualification | SE can flag "not ready for technical investment"; AE commits to addressing gaps before scheduling |
| Meeting roles | AE owns opening, closing, and commercial; SE owns technical content and discovery depth |
| Disagreements | Raised privately, never in front of customers; escalated to manager if unresolved after 1 attempt |
| CRM logging | Both parties log notes within 24 hours of every customer interaction |

🟡 **Mid-Level** — The best AE/SE partnerships aren't conflict-free. They're conflict-productive — where friction generates better deal strategy instead of resentment.

## 1.2.2 Managing the Commission-Breath AE

"Commission-Breath" is the industry term for an AE who is so desperate to close a deal that their desperation becomes visible — to you, to the prospect, and eventually to the competitor who capitalises on it. Commission-breath AEs don't make bad decisions because they're bad salespeople. They make bad decisions because their quota pressure has overwhelmed their judgement. Your job isn't to judge them. It's to protect the deal from their anxiety.

1. **Recognise the signs early.** Commission-breath manifests as: pushing for demos before discovery is complete ("just show them something, we're running out of time"), insisting on discounting before the prospect has even asked ("let's lead with 30% off to make sure we win"), fabricating urgency ("the prospect says they're deciding Friday" — when no such statement was made), and scheduling too many meetings ("let's do daily check-ins with the champion"). These are stress behaviours, not strategy.

2. **Anchor them with a deal plan.** The best antidote to commission-breath is a written deal plan that sequences the next 6–8 steps with specific dates and owners. When the AE says "let's just demo tomorrow," you can point to the plan: "Our agreed next step is to complete the technical discovery with their infrastructure team on Thursday. Demoing before that will expose us to requirements we haven't surfaced yet." Plans give both of you an objective reference point that's harder to override with emotion.

3. **Protect the prospect from the pressure.** If the AE's desperation is becoming visible to the prospect — overeager follow-ups, gratuitous discounting, manufactured deadlines — you need to intervene as the "calm professional" on your team. In customer meetings, take the temperature down. "We want to make sure this evaluation is thorough and meets your team's standards. Let's walk through the architecture questions your team raised." You're signalling to the prospect that at least one person on your side is focused on their needs rather than your internal deadline.

4. **Have the conversation privately, once.** If the behaviour is persistent, address it directly — once, clearly, and without accusation: "I'm concerned that if we push for a decision before they've finished their security review, we'll lose credibility. Can we align on a timeline that matches their process?" If the AE adjusts, great. If they don't, escalate to your SE manager. Do not continue to fight this battle on every deal — escalation exists for a reason.

5. **Understand when the pressure comes from above.** Sometimes the AE isn't the source. Their sales manager is pressuring them to commit deals they know aren't ready. The AE is caught between a demand they can't refuse and a reality they can't change. In these cases, empathy matters. Aligned pushback from both AE and SE to the sales leadership — "we both believe this deal is Q2, not Q1, and here's the evidence" — is far more effective than the SE fighting the AE.

> **War Story:** An AE was 60% to quota with 3 weeks left in the quarter. He had one deal that could get him to 90%. The SE saw the prospect's procurement team hadn't even begun their security review — a process that typically took 4–6 weeks at that company. The AE insisted on "getting the deal to paper" by submitting a deeply discounted proposal. The SE pushed back privately: "If we discount now before security clears us, we set a price anchor we can't recover from, and we still won't close this quarter because procurement hasn't started." The AE didn't listen. He submitted the proposal at 40% off list. Procurement took 7 weeks. The deal closed the following quarter — at the discounted price. The company left $180K on the table because the AE's anxiety set the price 8 weeks too early.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who enables commission-breath because they want to be seen as "a team player." They agree to demo before discovery. They help fabricate urgency by sending "limited-time pricing" emails they know aren't real. They co-author proposals that overclaim capabilities to create a sense of momentum. In the short term, the AE loves them. In the long term, they're complicit in a pattern that loses deals, damages customer relationships, and builds a reputation for dishonesty.

**Interview Angle:**
"Tell me about a time an AE wanted to move faster than you thought was appropriate. How did you handle it?"
A strong answer shows the SE anchoring to process (deal plan, qualification data), communicating directly with the AE, and protecting the deal outcome even at the cost of short-term friction. A weak answer prioritises "keeping the peace" over deal quality.

**Practical Reference: The Commission-Breath De-Escalation Script**
When the AE proposes a premature action, use this framework:

*"I hear you — I know the timeline pressure is real. Here's my concern: if we [proposed premature action], we risk [specific negative outcome]. What I'd recommend instead is [alternative action] by [specific date]. That gets us to the same place, but with the prospect's trust intact. Does that work?"*

This script validates the AE's pressure, names the risk, proposes an alternative, and sets a date. It's collaborative, not confrontational.

🟡 **Mid-Level** — Managing commission-breath is a diplomacy exercise. You're protecting the deal, the prospect, and the AE — often from themselves. Get comfortable with productive discomfort.

## 1.2.3 When to Overrule Your AE

This section will make some AEs uncomfortable. Good. Because the SE who never overrules their AE isn't a partner — they're an order-taker. And order-takers don't protect deals. Order-takers protect relationships with AEs at the expense of relationships with prospects.

There are specific, limited situations where the SE must overrule the AE's judgement. Doing it wisely is what separates a respected senior SE from a political casualty.

1. **Overrule when the AE is making a false technical claim.** If the AE tells a prospect "yes, we support real-time CDC from SAP HANA to Snowflake natively" and you know that's not true, you cannot let that stand. Not because you want to embarrass the AE. Because if the prospect builds their evaluation around a capability you don't have, you will lose the deal later — and lose it badly, with the prospect feeling lied to. The technique: don't contradict in the meeting. Instead, add precision: "To make sure we're accurate here — our platform handles the CDC pipeline, but the SAP connector requires a specific configuration for HANA. Let me send the exact specs after this call so your team can validate fit." You've corrected the record without calling the AE a liar.

2. **Overrule when the AE wants to skip discovery.** "They already know what they want — just demo it." This sentence has killed more deals than any competitor. If the AE pushes for a demo without discovery, and you proceed, you'll demo blind — showing features that may or may not map to the prospect's actual requirements. The result is a generic, forgettable presentation that gives the prospect no reason to choose you over anyone else. Hold the line: "I can demo Thursday, but I need 30 minutes with their technical lead first to make sure what I show is relevant. Otherwise we're guessing."

3. **Overrule when the AE is scoping a POC you can't win.** Sometimes an AE agrees to POC terms that are designed to favour a competitor: criteria that test capabilities where the competitor is strongest, timelines that are too aggressive for your team, or scope that includes roadmap features as if they're shipping. If you proceed, you're setting up a public failure. The overrule here is pre-emptive: "Before I commit to this POC, I need to review the success criteria with our team. I want to make sure we can deliver on every line item — if there's something we can't do today, I need to flag it now, not in week 3."

4. **Overrule when the AE is ignoring a deal-killing risk.** You've flagged a data sovereignty requirement. The AE waved it off: "They'll work around it." You've flagged that the prospect is also evaluating a competitor who has a significant advantage in one area. The AE dismissed it: "They're not a real threat." When the AE is ignoring a risk that you believe will kill the deal, document your concern in writing (CRM note, email, Slack) and escalate to your SE manager if the AE won't engage. The documentation protects you when the deal is lost and the post-mortem asks, "Did anyone see this coming?"

5. **Never overrule the AE on commercial terms.** The line is clear: you own the technical truth, the AE owns the commercial relationship. If the AE wants to offer a 25% discount and you think it's too generous — that's their call. If the AE wants to position the deal as a multi-year commitment and you prefer a shorter pilot — that's their call. Overruling on commercial matters is a boundary violation that will cost you the AE's trust permanently.

> **War Story:** An AE told a prospect that their platform could "replace Informatica entirely" in a data integration evaluation. The SE knew this was a dangerous overclaim — the product could replace about 60% of Informatica's functionality but had no equivalent for several advanced transformation patterns the prospect was using. The SE didn't correct the AE in the meeting. After the call, he sent the AE a message: "I want to make sure we don't over-expose ourselves. We're strong on ETL and real-time streaming but weak on complex transformation orchestration. If they test us on Informatica's PowerCenter-style workflows, we'll fail visibly. Can we reframe as 'complementary to Informatica for modern workloads' instead of 'replacement for Informatica'?" The AE adjusted the positioning. The deal closed — scoped to the workloads where they actually won. If the SE had stayed silent, the POC would have exposed the gap publicly, and the prospect would have lost trust in both the product and the team.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who overrules the AE publicly, in front of the prospect. "Actually, that's not how our product works." Three words. One destroyed partnership. The AE looks incompetent. The SE looks insubordinate. The prospect loses confidence in the team. The correct instinct — protecting technical truth — was executed with the wrong technique. Always correct privately. Always add nuance publicly.

**Interview Angle:**
"Describe a situation where you disagreed with your AE's approach to a deal. What did you do?"
A strong answer describes a specific disagreement, the SE's reasoning, how they communicated it (privately, with data), and the outcome. A weak answer either avoids the topic ("we always agreed") or describes a confrontation without a resolution.

**Practical Reference: The Overrule Decision Matrix**

| Situation | Should You Overrule? | How? |
|-----------|---------------------|------|
| AE makes a false technical claim | Yes, always | Add precision publicly; correct privately |
| AE wants to skip discovery | Yes | Insist on minimum 30-min technical call before demo |
| AE agrees to unfavourable POC terms | Yes | Review criteria before committing; flag risks in writing |
| AE ignores a deal-killing risk | Yes | Document in CRM; escalate to SE manager if unaddressed |
| AE offers an aggressive discount | No | Their domain — raise concerns privately if appropriate |
| AE chooses a deal strategy you disagree with | Depends | Share your perspective with data; defer to them if they're unconvinced |

🟡 **Mid-Level** — The right to overrule is earned. SEs who have a track record of correct judgment get more latitude. Build that track record by being right more often than wrong — and by being graceful when you're wrong.

## 1.2.4 Establishing Deal Swimlanes That Actually Hold

Every SE/AE partnership needs swimlanes — defined areas of ownership that prevent stepping on each other's toes, eliminate duplicated effort, and create a unified customer experience. The problem isn't that teams don't have swimlanes. The problem is that the swimlanes are vague, undocumented, and violated the moment pressure increases.

Swimlanes that "actually hold" are explicitly agreed upon, documented, and enforced — especially during deal crises when the temptation to abandon them is strongest.

1. **The AE owns the opening and closing. The SE owns the middle.** The simplest swimlane definition: the AE opens relationships, sets commercial context, explores budget, and drives the contract to signature. The SE owns discovery, solution design, demonstration, POC, and technical validation. The grey zone — where most conflicts occur — is the transition between discovery and proposal. Both parties think they own "shaping the solution for the prospect." Define who leads each part explicitly.

2. **The AE owns stakeholder access. The SE owns stakeholder trust.** The AE should be the one requesting introductions to the economic buyer, setting up meetings with procurement, and navigating the political landscape. The SE should be the one earning technical credibility in those meetings through depth, preparation, and precision. When an SE starts scheduling meetings independently without the AE's knowledge, the partnership fractures. When an AE gatekeeps the SE from talking to technical evaluators, the deal suffers.

3. **Meeting roles should be pre-agreed, not improvised.** Before every customer meeting, spend 5 minutes aligning: "I'll open with context and introductions. You take discovery from minute 5. I'll handle questions about pricing and next steps. You handle all technical questions." This prevents the two most common meeting dysfunctions: the AE and SE talking over each other (the prospect watches two people compete for airtime), and dead silence when neither person thinks they're supposed to respond.

4. **Document the swimlanes for every deal, not just the team.** Generic team-level swimlanes break down because every deal is different. A deal with a strong champion requires different AE/SE coordination than a deal where you're fighting uphill against a detractor. At the start of every deal, co-create a brief deal plan that includes role assignments for each milestone: "AE leads champion coaching. SE leads technical evaluation. Both attend executive briefing. AE leads proposal walkthrough. SE leads POC execution."

5. **Enforce swimlanes when they're violated, not after the deal is lost.** The first time an AE makes a technical claim they shouldn't have, address it that day. The first time an SE sends a commercial email without the AE's input, address it that day. Small violations that go uncorrected become cultural norms. Cultural norms become deal-killing habits. Enforcement isn't confrontation — it's a 3-minute conversation: "Hey, I noticed you jumped into the architecture discussion during the call. I know it came naturally, but that's my lane — if I'm not leading the technical narrative, the prospect gets inconsistent depth. Can we keep to the plan next time?"

> **War Story:** An AE and SE had an unspoken swimlane agreement: the AE handled "business conversations" and the SE handled "technical conversations." The problem surfaced when the prospect's CTO asked a question that was both: "If we go with your platform, what's the business case for replacing our current system — how do we justify the migration cost?" The AE looked at the SE. The SE looked at the AE. Three seconds of silence. The CTO said, "Does anyone want to take that?" Both answered simultaneously with conflicting framings. The CTO lost confidence in the team's alignment. After the meeting, the AE and SE sat down and redefined their swimlanes with a new rule: "If a question bridges business and technical, the SE provides the technical substance and the AE wraps it in commercial context." No more silence. No more collisions.

> **Failure Mode:** The SE who resists swimlanes because they view them as constraints on their autonomy. "I should be able to talk about anything in the meeting — I'm not going to stay silent when I have something valuable to add." This SE consistently talks over the AE, answers commercial questions they shouldn't be answering, and creates a meeting dynamic where the prospect hears two competing narratives. The AE eventually requests a different SE. Not because the SE was wrong — but because working with them was exhausting.

**Interview Angle:**
"How do you define roles and responsibilities with your AE partner on a deal?"
A strong answer describes a specific, pre-agreed framework (deal plans with role assignments, pre-meeting alignment calls, documented swimlanes). A weak answer says "we just figure it out as we go."

**Practical Reference: The Deal Swimlane Template**

| Deal Stage | AE Leads | SE Leads | Shared |
|-----------|----------|----------|--------|
| Initial qualification | Budget, authority, timeline | Technical fit assessment | Champion identification |
| Discovery | Business pain, org dynamics | Technical architecture, integrations, security | Pain quantification |
| Demo | Opening, agenda, commercial framing | Product narrative, technical depth, objection handling | Next step commitment |
| POC | Executive sponsorship, resource commitment | Criteria design, execution, daily standups | Mid-POC checkpoint with champion |
| Proposal | Pricing, terms, commercial negotiation | Technical scope, architecture documentation | Final presentation |
| Procurement | Contract red-lining, legal negotiation | Security questionnaires, technical addenda | Timeline management |

Print this. Fill it in for each deal. Review it when conflicts arise. It takes 10 minutes to complete and prevents weeks of friction.

🟡 **Mid-Level** — Swimlanes are not about control. They're about predictability. A prospect who interacts with a well-coordinated AE/SE pair feels like they're working with a team. A prospect who interacts with an uncoordinated pair feels like they're watching an internal argument.
